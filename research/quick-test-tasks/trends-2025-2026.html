What is this scan for, and what isn't it for?

This is the tie-breaker only input to quick-test task selection — it does not judge rubric fit. The quick-test rubric does that, and the downstream scoring agent must cross this against the rubric independently. Every area below was first filtered to be plausibly rubric-compatible (cheap, single-call, exact-scored, synthetic-friendly, MC or few-words output), then scored for one question only: is this an active 2025-2026 research area? Areas are ranked by strength and directness of the tie-breaker signal, not by rubric fit.

Areas 1-3 win the tie-breaker. Verifiable instruction-following, constraint satisfaction, and compositional generalization have the strongest, most current, most citable 2025-2026 activity and the closest structural match to the rubric's own mechanism (headroom plus incremental, decomposable, dial-able rules). Areas 4-6 (in-context rule learning, rule induction, cipher decoding) form a coherent second tier — smaller but clearly active, and arguably a tighter conceptual fit for a synthetic, parameterized, exact-match, few-shot-sensitive task. Area 9 shows that whichever task is chosen, publishing it as a small prompt-optimizer benchmark is itself an active, receptive line of work right now.

What are the nine active research areas?

1 · Verifiable / constraint-based instruction-following

Very highThe most heavily benchmarked LLM-eval subfield in 2025-2026, precisely because it satisfies exact, code-verifiable scoring (word counts, keyword inclusion, formatting) at low cost — the same properties this rubric wants. Direct successor to IFEval (2023); frontier models saturated the original, spawning a small industry of harder follow-ups.

  • IFEval → IFBench (Pyatkin et al., 2025): 58 new unseen verifiable constraint types, built because models overfit IFEval's known set — direct evidence of the "incremental gap, not one-shot saturable" property.
  • CFBench (2025): 1,000 samples, 200+ scenarios, 10 primary constraint categories, 25+ subcategories, multi-dimensional constraint-priority scoring.
  • FollowBench / ComplexBench: increase difficulty by adding constraints level by level — explicit decomposable-difficulty design, close to rubric criterion 10.
  • SysBench (2025, arXiv 2408.10943): system-message adherence.
  • TRUEBench, CCR-Bench, LsrIF (2025-2026): constraint/format-compliance for real-world instructions.
  • BenchLM.ai instruction-following leaderboard actively tracked through July 2026.
  • IF-RewardBench (2026): benchmarks judge models on instruction-following — the area has matured enough to spawn meta-evaluation.

Signal: very strong and current — new papers essentially monthly through mid-2026, plus a live leaderboard culture.

2 · Constraint satisfaction / structured-output compliance

HighOverlaps area 1 but emphasizes compositional constraints and optimization-style verification (e.g. against solvers) — a natural bridge to prompt-optimization research, since constraint count is a tunable difficulty knob.

  • ConstraintBench (2026, arXiv 2602.22465): constrained optimization across 10 OR domains, verified against the Gurobi solver — exact programmatic ground truth by construction.
  • CCR-Bench (2026): complex constraints plus control flow plus real-world cases.
  • "Can LLMs Reason and Optimize Under Constraints?" (2026, arXiv 2603.23004).
  • FineCS and related fine-grained benchmarks track performance as constraint count scales — a controllable-difficulty pattern.

Signal: strong; slightly more niche/academic than area 1 but clearly active into 2026. Its "add one constraint at a time" framing maps almost one-to-one onto rubric criterion 10 (independent latent rules) and criterion 4 (incremental gap-closing).

3 · Compositional generalization

High2025 produced a wave of benchmarks measuring the gap between "solves sub-tasks individually" and "solves the composed task" — structurally the same thing prompt optimizers exploit.

  • "Revisiting Compositional Generalization Capability of LLMs Considering Instruction-Following Ability" (ACL 2025, arXiv 2506.15629).
  • Ordered CommonGen (2025): concept-ordering; best model reaches only ~75% ordered coverage — clean "default sits below ceiling," synthetic and exact-scorable.
  • CryptoX (2025): cryptographic encoding forces subproblem decomposition; a compositional-AUC gap between closed- and open-source models. Overlaps cipher tasks (area 6).
  • AgentCoMa (2025): mixed commonsense+math composition; ~30% compositionality gap — models ace sub-tasks but fail the combination (rubric criterion 10).
  • DafnyCOMP (2025): compositional formal verification; pass rate collapses from >90% (single function) to <5% (multi-function) — a dramatic, well-documented incremental-difficulty curve.

Signal: strong; arguably the most conceptually aligned area with the rubric's core mechanism (criteria 4 and 10). 2025 was clearly active (ACL, multiple new benchmarks).

4 · In-context rule learning / synthetic formal-language ICL

Moderate-highA resurgence of controlled, synthetic-environment studies of whether models truly learn in context versus pattern-match to pretraining. Ties to the academic ICL-theory community (GINC, RegBench) and fits a task where few-shot demos should measurably help (rubric criterion 12).

  • Rashid (2026, arXiv 2603.22497): "A Cipher-Based Framework for Exploring In-Context Language Learning" — purpose-built synthetic cipher/ICL framework.
  • GINC and RegBench-style synthetic formal-language datasets remain the standard controlled substrate for ICL theory.
  • 2025-2026 surveys call "Context Learning" (learning genuinely new information from context rather than recalling pretraining) "a severe bottleneck for contemporary LLMs" — active framing as of 2026.
  • "Task Vectors in In-Context Learning: Emergence, Formation, and Benefit" (2025), part of a broader mechanistic-interpretability-meets-ICL thread.

Signal: moderate-strong; smaller community than areas 1-3 but clearly active, with fresh 2026 papers. Tied to "does few-shot help" (criterion 12) and contamination-resistance (criterion 8).

5 · Rule induction / hypothesis search from few-shot examples

Moderate-highDistinct but adjacent to area 4, focused on models inferring a hidden transformation rule from I/O pairs and applying it — structurally identical to "latent rules + few-shot demos + exact match" (rubric criteria 10, 12).

  • "Analysis of Error Sources in LLM-based Hypothesis Search for Few-Shot Rule Induction" (2025, arXiv 2509.01016): string-transformation rule inference from limited I/O; compares hypothesis-search vs direct-generation.
  • MIR-Bench (2025, arXiv 2502.09933): many-shot in-context inductive reasoning over function-based I/O — directly probes criterion 12's premise.
  • FalsifyBench (2026, arXiv 2606.04751): "Evaluating Inductive Reasoning in LLMs with Rule Discovery Games."
  • WILT (2024, extended into 2025): Wason 2-4-6-inspired multi-turn inductive reasoning.
  • Linguistics-Olympiad-style: LINGOLY, Linguini, IOLBench (2024-2025) — rule discovery in low-resource/synthetic-like languages, contamination-resistant and decomposable into independent grammatical rules.

Signal: moderate-strong; smaller and more academic than areas 1-3, but multiple 2025-2026 papers and a clean match to criteria 8, 10, 11 (diagnostic failures reveal which rule was missed).

6 · Cipher / symbolic decoding tasks

ModerateA crisp, easily-synthesizable exact-match family that 2025 papers use to show models fail on novel (non-memorized) bijective mappings while succeeding on famous ones (Caesar shift-3, Morse) — built-in contamination diagnostics.

  • CipherBench, CipherBank (2025): common/uncommon/novel cipher categories; models only handle ciphers frequent in pretraining — a quotable contamination-resistance argument (criterion 8) and evidence for headroom (criterion 4).
  • "Benchmarking LLMs for Cryptanalysis and Mismatched-Generalization" (EMNLP Findings 2025, arXiv 2505.24621): 4,509-entry dataset, 9 encryption algorithms; exact-match failures distinct from BLEU-style partial credit (criterion 2).
  • CryptoX (2025, cross-ref area 3): reuses cipher/encoding as a compositionality-forcing mechanism.
  • Rashid (2026, cross-ref area 4): cipher framework specifically for ICL.

Signal: moderate; smaller/nicher than areas 1-3, but multiple concrete 2025-2026 papers and a very direct structural fit (single call, exact match, synthetic and parameterizable, latent "rule" = the cipher key).

7 · Abstract/algorithmic rule-induction on grid or symbolic puzzles (ARC-family)

High ecosystem, niche direct fit. ARC-AGI remains one of the most visible "not solved by scaling alone" benchmarks — an active prize, a 2025 harder version, a growing academic ecosystem into 2026. More relevant as inspiration than direct fit (ARC itself is multi-step/visual, heavier than this rubric wants), but the "synthetic verifiable puzzle" trend it anchors is on-rubric.

  • ARC-AGI-2 (2025 ARC Prize) and ARC-AGI-3 (announced 2026, adds interactive elements) — sustained, funded, high-visibility competition.
  • ARC-GEN (2025, arXiv 2511.00162): procedural benchmark generator mimicking ARC-AGI — evidences "synthetic, parameterized, generate-with-known-ground-truth" as an active pattern.
  • Enigmata / Enigmata-Eval (2025, arXiv 2505.19914): a trained model (Qwen2.5-32B-Enigmata) reported beating o1/o3-mini-high on puzzle benchmarks.
  • LogicBench, ZebraLogic, AutoLogic (2024-2025): symbolic logic puzzles with controllable interacting-constraint complexity.
  • CogniLoad (2025, arXiv 2509.18458): synthetic NL reasoning with tunable length, intrinsic difficulty, and distractor density — a textbook match for "difficulty decomposes into independent, dial-able rules."

Signal: strong at the ecosystem level (funded prize, multiple 2025-2026 generator papers) even though the flagship ARC task is heavier than this rubric wants; the tunable-synthetic-puzzle-generator sub-trend is the directly relevant part.

8 · Agent / tool-use micro-benchmarks (single function-call scoring)

Very high industry activity. Tool-calling accuracy is one of the most commercially-driven 2025-2026 eval areas, keeping leaderboards live and frequently updated — useful signal even though most work targets multi-turn agents rather than single-call tasks.

  • Berkeley Function-Calling Leaderboard (BFCL) v4 (Gorilla project): actively maintained; scores single-call argument/API-selection precision — close to this rubric's "single LLM call, exact check" shape.
  • MCPToolBench++ (2025-2026): thousands of live MCP servers, multi-step and cross-domain, positioned as the next-gen successor once BFCL/tau-bench felt saturated for frontier models.
  • Scale Labs "Agentic Tool Use" leaderboard, τ-bench: actively tracked.

Signal: moderate; very active as an industry eval area, but the frontier of interest (MCP, multi-turn, live servers) is drifting away from the simple single-call shape this rubric wants.

9 · Prompt-optimization benchmarking itself (GEPA / MIPROv2 / DSPy ecosystem)

High, self-referential. Directly relevant to whetstone-ai's own method space — the field the optimizers under test belong to, and unusually active right now.

  • GEPA ("Reflective Prompt Evolution Can Outperform RL," arXiv 2507.19457) — accepted as an oral at ICLR 2026; reports GEPA beating MIPROv2 by 13% aggregate (+12% on AIME 2025) and beating GRPO-style RL by up to 20% while using up to 35x fewer rollouts.
  • MAS-PromptBench (2026, arXiv 2606.23664): "When Does Prompt Optimization Improve Multi-Agent LLM Systems?" — a benchmark for comparing optimizers, exactly whetstone-ai's own use case.
  • Automated Instruction Revision (AIR) (2026, arXiv 2604.09418): structured comparison of task-adaptation/prompt-optimization strategies.
  • DD-GEPA (2026, arXiv 2606.07894): domain-specific GEPA variant — the method is being actively forked less than a year after release.

Signal: strong and directly self-referential — if the quick test also functions as a new small optimizer benchmark, it slots straight into this active line, and picking a task from areas 1-6 is mutually reinforcing.
