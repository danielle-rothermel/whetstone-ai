Generator-native rule-induction tasks need the least adaptation to fit the rubric

Twelve candidates for synthetic rule-induction and compositional generalization, judged against the quick-test rubric.

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-21
Quick-test rubric Recommendations Landscape Rule induction

What does the rubric demand of a rule-induction task?

A breadth-focused scan for tasks, benchmarks, and dataset generators that could be used or adapted as the quick-test task for the whetstone-ai prompt-optimizer harness (COPRO, MIPROv2, GEPA, Codex agent), judged against the quick-test rubric — the authority for every verdict below. Twelve candidates plus adjacent leads, not ranked.

  • C1/C2/C3 (cheap) — single LLM call, exact deterministic programmatic scoring (no sandbox), output bounded to multiple-choice or a few words / short token sequence.
  • C4 (incremental gap) — default prompt scores well below a known ceiling, and the gap closes in steps; the best prompt is not one-shot guessable by a competent prompt engineer.
  • C5 (resolvable at n=10-20, temp 0) — effect size of a ≥10pt prompt change must exceed residual noise on a tiny eval.
  • C7/C8 (synthetic, hundreds of instances, contamination-resistant) — program-generated with exact ground truth.
  • C9 — known ceiling/floor plus a reference prompt.
  • C10 — difficulty decomposes into independent latent rules; which rules a prompt found is inspectable.
  • C11 — failures diagnostic for a reflection LM.
  • C12 — few-shot demos measurably help.
  • C13 — failure paths exercised (parse errors, empty generation).
The most rubric-aligned families are generator-native rule-induction tasks. Five candidates — PCFG SET, InductionBench, List Functions, Boolean Concept Learning, and ICL Ciphers — already ship exact ground truth, a demo format, and a complexity dial, minimizing adaptation. Two universal fixes apply to nearly all candidates: bound output to one MC letter or short token (C2/C3), and re-seed the latent key per run (C8). Two traps recur: the ceiling-reachability trap (the hardest tasks may have no ~100% prompt, killing C4's headroom) and the one-shot-guessability trap (single ciphers, single-rule concepts, and "just use CoT" tasks are one-shot solvable).

In-context rule induction is unusually active right now — InductionBench (2025), ICL Ciphers (2025), HERO'S JOURNEY (2026), SCFG in-context transduction (2026), LingOly / Rosetta-to-Match-Up (2024-2026) — so a quick-test built on a synthetic rule-induction generator has a natural path to becoming its own research artifact. Because almost all published benchmarks are fixed corpora, contamination (C8) and one-shot-guessability (C4) are the two dimensions that most often need engineering; the winning move for most candidates is to keep the generator but discard the published split, re-key the latent rules per run, bound output to one MC letter or short token, and tune rule count so the naive-prompt floor sits well below the ceiling.

What are the twelve candidates?

1. SCAN / MiniSCAN

Compositional command → action grammar

What. Lake & Baroni's SCAN maps synthetic navigation commands ("jump twice and walk left") to action sequences via ~20 phrase-structure grammar rules over ~20 words. MiniSCAN shrinks it to a handful of invented primitives learned purely in-context.

Adaptation. Raw SCAN outputs are long action sequences (violates C2/C3). Convert to a single-step variant: give k demo pairs with an invented grammar, ask for only the first action token or a single MC among candidates. Regenerate the grammar per run to dodge C8.

Fit. Strong C7/C10/C12. Weaknesses: canonical SCAN is memorized (C8) and long-output (C2/C3) — both fixed only by the MiniSCAN-style rewrite.

Lake & Baroni, ICML 2018 (arXiv:1711.00350); least-to-most prompting on SCAN (arXiv:2205.10625, 2022).

2. gSCAN / ReaSCAN

Grounded grid-world compositional instructions

What. gSCAN grounds SCAN commands in a grid world; tests 8 compositional splits. ReaSCAN adds relational reasoning.

Adaptation. Full gSCAN wants an action sequence over a rendered grid — too heavy (C1/C2/C3 fail). Usable only collapsed to a textual single-question form: describe the grid in text, ask one MC.

Fit. Excellent C10/C7, but grid rendering plus sequence output make it the most expensive to bound — likely not worth the adaptation cost against lighter candidates.

Ruis et al., NeurIPS 2020 (arXiv:2003.05161); ReaSCAN (arXiv:2109.08994, 2021); code.

3. COGS / SLOG

Compositional semantic parsing

What. COGS maps procedurally-generated English sentences to logical forms; 21 generalization categories (18 lexical, 3 structural). SLOG extends structural generalization.

Adaptation. Output is a logical form — partial-credit temptation violates C2. Bound to exact-match of a short canonicalized form, or MC over candidate parses.

Fit. Good C7/C10; the 18-vs-3 lexical/structural split is a natural C4 knob. Weakness: exact-match on logical forms is brittle — long-ish outputs pressure C3.

Kim & Linzen, EMNLP 2020 (COGS); SLOG (arXiv:2604.26157).

4. PCFG SET

String-edit transduction, 5 named generalization axes

What. Sequences from a probabilistic CFG must be translated to their "meaning" via string-edit operators (copy, reverse, shift, echo, append…) across five axes: systematicity, productivity, substitutivity, localism, overgeneralization.

Adaptation. Fully synthetic generator already exists (strong C7/C8). Bound output to first-token or fixed-short-length, or MC; each operator is an independent latent rule (C10) and the five axes grade difficulty (C4).

Fit. One of the best structural fits: explicitly designed for independent, nameable rules with an incremental path, generator-native. Weakness: output-length bounding still required (C3).

Hupkes et al., JAIR 2020, "Compositionality Decomposed" (arXiv:1908.08351).

5. InductionBench generator-native

Subregular function/transducer induction — 2025

What. LLMs must induce the rule of a string-to-string function from the simplest subregular complexity classes (ISL/OSL/etc.), from I/O demos. Even o1/o3 fail the simplest classes.

Adaptation. Very close to ideal — native rule-induction from demos (C12), synthetic with exact ground truth (C7/C8), complexity-classed (C4/C10). Bound output to applying the induced rule to one held-out input (few-token exact match); re-generate keys per run.

Fit. Among the strongest overall (C4/C7/C8/C10/C11/C12). Weakness: "simplest classes still fail" means the ceiling may be hard to reach — must verify a reference prompt hits ≈100% on the easiest classes, else C4's headroom collapses.

Hua et al., "InductionBench" (arXiv:2502.15823, 2025).

6. List Functions generator-native

Program/concept induction over integer lists

What. 250 human-authored list→list functions (map, filter, sort, dedupe, arithmetic) — a gold standard for few-shot concept induction with human baselines; present in BIG-Bench as list_functions.

Adaptation. Give k input→output demos, ask for the output on one query (short exact-match, no sandbox — label precomputed, C2). Regenerate the parameters of each function family per run for C8 (the 250 concepts are published).

Fit. Strong C7/C10/C11/C12 and clean exact-match scoring. Weakness: some functions produce long list outputs (C3) — restrict to length-bounded families.

Rule PhD thesis (2020); Alet et al., ICML 2021; BIG-bench list_functions.

7. In-Context Boolean Concept Learning generator-native

Complexity-graded — 2024

What. LLMs learn a hidden Boolean concept over feature vectors from labeled in-context examples; accuracy correlates with the concept's Boolean complexity, mirroring the human simplicity bias.

Adaptation. Almost turnkey — output is a single YES/NO or MC label (C2/C3 native). Fully synthetic feature vectors plus Boolean formula give exact ground truth, unlimited instances, contamination-proof (C7/C8). Boolean complexity is a direct floor→ceiling knob (C4/C9).

Fit. Possibly the tightest fit to C1-C5 of any candidate. Weakness: binary label per item means C5 noise is high per-instance — needs enough query items per eval; failures are less individually diagnostic (C11 weaker).

"Minimization of Boolean Complexity in In-Context Concept Learning" (arXiv:2412.02823, 2024).

8. ICL Ciphers / Rashid generator-native

Substitution-cipher in-context learning — 2025-2026

What. Re-maps the token/vocabulary of a task via a bijective substitution cipher so the model must learn the mapping in-context rather than recall pretraining knowledge — built explicitly to be contamination-proof.

Adaptation. The cipher key IS the latent rule; regenerate per run solves C8 by construction. Bound to decoding one short ciphered token/answer, exact match. Compose multiple independent sub-ciphers for C10 decomposition and a graded floor→ceiling (C4).

Fit. Best-in-class on C8 and strong on C12. Weakness: a single monoalphabetic cipher is one-shot solvable once a prompt engineer names it (fails C4) — needs composed/keyed ciphers or hidden structure.

"ICL CIPHERS" (arXiv:2504.19395, 2025); "Rashid" (arXiv:2603.22497, 2026).

9. HERO'S JOURNEY

Complex rule induction in text games — 2026

What. Agents infer hidden rules from demonstrations in goal-directed episodic text games; 8 tasks across attribute-induction and procedural-induction families, each with 4 structural rule forms. Finds current LLM rule induction limited and uneven.

Adaptation. Multi-step execution violates C1/C2 as-is. Extract the single-decision form: show demos of the hidden rule, ask for one next action / MC. Controllable lexical grounding is the "swap surface to dodge contamination" knob (C8).

Fit. Very strong on C7/C8/C10/C11 and freshest research heat. Weakness: procedural family may be too hard (ceiling unreachable → C9/C4 risk); newest, so tooling is less mature.

Zheng, Misra, Beaver, Li (arXiv:2606.02556, June 2026).

10. Re-ARC / 1D-ARC / MiniARC / ARC-GEN

Procedural ARC-style grid rule induction

What. ARC = infer a grid transformation from 2-3 demos, apply to a test grid. Re-ARC is a procedural generator with configurable difficulty for all 400 training tasks; 1D-ARC linearizes to arrays (18 categories, 901 tasks); ARC-GEN (2025) is a mimetic procedural generator.

Adaptation. Full 2D-grid output is verbose and format-fragile (C3). Prefer 1D-ARC linearized to short arrays, output bounded to the transformed array or MC. Re-ARC's difficulty dial plus infinite generation directly serve C4/C7/C8.

Fit. Excellent C7/C8 and C10. Weaknesses: grid serialization inflates tokens; ARC is famously hard, so ceiling reachability is a real risk unless restricted to easy 1D transforms.

Chollet ARC (arXiv:1911.01547); Re-ARC (arXiv:2404.07353, 2024, code); 1D-ARC (arXiv:2305.18354); ARC-GEN (arXiv:2511.00162, 2025).

11. BIG-Bench Hard — symbolic subset

Boolean expressions, logical deduction, tracking shuffled objects, Dyck languages, web-of-lies

What. 23-27 hard BBH tasks; several are algorithmically generatable with MC/short answers: boolean_expressions, logical_deduction, dyck_languages, tracking_shuffled_objects, web_of_lies, multistep_arithmetic.

Adaptation. Use only the synthetic subset and regenerate instances from the underlying generators (published BBH is contaminated). Many are already MC/short-token. Rules decompose per-task (ordering constraints, swap-tracking); CoT vs. direct is a known incremental-headroom lever (C4).

Fit. Strong C1/C2/C3, good C4, decent C10. Weakness: heavy contamination as published — must regenerate; latent-rule decomposition is per-task and less clean than the grammar/cipher families.

Suzgun et al., "Challenging BIG-Bench Tasks" (arXiv:2210.09261, 2022).

12. LingOly / Rosetta-to-Match-Up

Rosetta-stone linguistic puzzles — 2024-2026

What. Olympiad linguistic puzzles: from paired examples in a low-resource/invented language, induce morphology/syntax rules and translate. LingOly (1,133 UKLO puzzles, NeurIPS 2024) reports SOTA ~21.7% exact-match.

Adaptation. Published puzzles are fixed and partly contaminated. Use a synthetic conlang generator instead of UKLO corpora; ask to translate one short word/phrase. Morpheme rules are independent latent rules (C10); rule count grades difficulty (C4).

Fit. Very strong C4 (low SOTA = huge headroom), C10, C12. Weakness: hand-built puzzles fail C7/C8 — the whole value depends on building a synthetic conlang generator; exact-match on morphology is noisy (C5).

Bean et al., "LINGOLY" (arXiv:2406.06196, NeurIPS 2024); "From Rosetta to Match-Up" (arXiv:2605.13408, 2026).

What are the adjacent leads, short of a full candidate?

  • Synchronous CFG in-context transduction (arXiv:2604.07320, 2026): evaluates ICL by translating between two formal languages defined by a synchronous CFG — a clean, compositional, contamination-proof generator overlapping #4/#5.
  • "Understanding the Relationship between ICL and Compositional Generalization" (arXiv:2403.11834, 2024): methodological grounding for why demos help compositional tasks (supports C12 design choices).
  • Grammar prompting for DSL generation (arXiv:2305.19234): BNF-constrained output — a technique to bound outputs (helps C2/C3/C13) rather than a task itself.
  • ConlangCrafter (arXiv:2508.06094, 2025) and "Creating ConLangs to Probe Metalinguistic Knowledge" (arXiv:2510.07591, 2025): generators that would make #12 synthetic.

What should the harness designer do with this?

Prioritize the generator-native five

#4 PCFG SET, #5 InductionBench, #6 List Functions, #7 Boolean Concept Learning, #8 ICL Ciphers already have exact ground truth, a demo format, and a complexity dial (C4/C7/C8/C10/C12). They minimize adaptation — favor them for a first prototype.

Apply the two universal adaptations

Bound output to one MC letter / one short token for C2/C3, and re-seed the latent key per run for C8. Almost every candidate needs both.

Watch the ceiling-reachability trap (C9)

The freshest/hardest tasks — InductionBench, HERO'S JOURNEY procedural, full ARC — may have no prompt that reaches ~100%, killing C4's "close the gap" story. Restrict to the easy end of each complexity hierarchy and verify a reference prompt hits ~100% before adopting.

Watch the one-shot-guessability trap (C4)

Single monoalphabetic ciphers, single-rule concepts, and "just use chain-of-thought" tasks are one-shot solvable by a competent prompt engineer. Use composed independent rules so partial prompts earn partial score and the best prompt is genuinely non-obvious.

Failure-path coverage (C13) comes free from sequence/array outputs (parse failures happen naturally); pure-MC tasks (#7, #11) may need deliberate malformed-output injection. See the Landscape for how these families map onto the ranked candidate pool, and Recommendations for the adoption case.
