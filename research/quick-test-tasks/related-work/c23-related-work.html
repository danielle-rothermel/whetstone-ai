c23 Related Work: Hidden-Rule String-Transform Induction Ladder

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-22

Six papers bound c23's design space. Only InductionBench directly tests c23's exact substrate (subregular ISL/OSL string-transform induction) and doubles as its vendored codebase — and its own headline finding is a reachability warning: frontier models score 0.00–5.69% compatibility at moderate difficulty. MIR-Bench is the closest single-call neighbor on protocol but has no decomposable partial-credit structure; PCFG SET donates the operator taxonomy but uses named, not latent, rules; WILT and FalsifyBench are multi-turn relatives whose interactive confounds (doom-looping, confirmation bias) c23's single-call design structurally avoids; Re-ARC is a tooling analogue with no model results at all. The convergent warning: c23 sits in a genuinely empty cell (single-call × apply-to-held-out-query × composed latent rules), but that cell is empty partly because keeping its ceiling reachable is hard.

Paper dossiers

InductionBench: frontier models fail the simplest complexity class, and more evidence can make it worse

2502.15823
Hua, Wong, Fei, Pan, Jardine, Wang · ACL 2025 main conference (long paper), ACL Anthology 2025.acl-long.1287; arXiv v1 2025-02-20, v4 2025-05-13

Claim & novelty

Tests whether LLMs can induce a string-to-string transformation from a sample of I/O pairs, grounded in the subregular hierarchy (ISL, L-OSL, R-OSL classes, provably learnable in polynomial time from polynomially-sized samples). Central finding: even the strongest model (o3-mini) struggles to master these simplest complexity classes, and performance sometimes degrades rather than improves as more theoretically-helpful data is provided. Novelty: a formally grounded "characteristic sample" guaranteed sufficient for learnability, precision/recall/compatibility scoring against the unique minimal rule set (not just correctness), and dynamic/refreshable generation for anti-memorization. This is c23's central design driver and vendored codebase source.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot, zero-shot CoT: one prompt gives the function class, context window k, alphabet Σ, and a sample I/O dataset (the "characteristic sample," scaled 1x–5x); the model must emit a full rule-set hypothesis in the paper's own notation ("condition ∘ target → output"), not apply a rule to one held-out query. A separate few-shot sweep (0/1/2/3-shot demos) stays within this same single-call format. Compatibility is checked against the training sample itself, not a genuinely held-out item.

Models & headline numbers

  • At k=4, |Σ|=4, rules=3: compatibility collapses to 0.00 for all 6 models except o3-mini (10.00/10.00/30.00 for ISL/L-OSL/R-OSL).
  • Standard leaderboard (linear weight): best model o3-mini scores only 5.69% weighted compatibility — log-weighted improves to 33.93%, since k=4 dominates and disproportionately penalizes every model.
  • Context window k is the single largest difficulty driver — more so than vocabulary size — moving k from 2 to 4 "markedly reduces recall, precision, and compatibility across all models."
  • At easy ISL (k=2, vocab=2, rules=1): GPT-4o reaches compatibility 40; Llama-3.3-70B reaches 60 (0-shot) rising to 80 (3-shot) — the partial-traction regime c23's single-rule restriction targets.
  • More sample data can hurt: compatibility "decreases steeply" as I/O examples grow beyond the minimal characteristic sample, plummeting near zero at 4x–5x — driven by genuinely new information, not context length (repeating the same sample causes only a small decline).

Failure modes

  • Missing rules are most often completely missed or too general; wrong rules are most often completely wrong or too general — "too specific" errors are rare, suggesting that once a model identifies the right condition it usually gets the transformation right.
  • Adding a second or third rule can cause compatibility to plummet to near 0, especially at large k/vocab.
  • Robustness failure interpreted by the authors as the model becoming "confused or overwhelmed" by more data rather than reasoning "robustly inductive[ly]."

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. The paper is precisely subregular string-function induction with exactly the reachability caveat claimed (title: "LLMs Fail in the Simplest Complexity Class"), and the codebase (github.com/Wenyueh/inductive_reasoning_benchmark) is confirmed reachable. One nuance carried into the positioning analysis below: InductionBench's "I/O demos" mean the entire sample the model must explain, not a few-shot-demo-plus-held-out-query format — the whole sample IS the input, and the model must state the general rule, not apply it to a new item. This is the load-bearing protocol gap c23's design closes.

MIR-Bench: no model saturates many-shot pattern recognition, and no-CoT unanimously beats forced CoT

2502.09933
Yan, Ling, Liu, Yang, Fan, Shen, Du, Chen · NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks Track; arXiv v1 2025-02-14 – v5 2025-10-23 (camera-ready)

Claim & novelty

The first large-scale many-shot (4–2048 examples) in-context pattern-recognition benchmark: predict y_new = f(x_new) from n I/O pairs generated by an unknown Python function, across diverse data types. Central claim: all 21 tested LLMs fail to fully exploit many-shot context — performance generally saturates by ~256 shots, well before the 128K context limit, attributed to attention dispersion rather than retrieval failure. Novelty: functions mined from HumanEval+/MBPP+/APPS with fresh GPT-4o-written data generators (no data-leakage reuse of existing I/O pairs), plus six research questions (scaling, inductive vs. transductive, error robustness, code-based induction, RAG, meta-shot transfer) not jointly examined by prior work.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one call sees all n demonstrations plus one query and must output y_new in one forward response, ending with "Output: your answer" in strict Python format, extracted via rule-based parsing and scored by exact match. Demo count (4→2048) is the manipulated variable, not a conversation. Structurally the same output shape as c23: one derived value, exact-match scored.

Models & headline numbers

  • Best model (o1-mini) reaches <0.7 accuracy on MIR-Extended; most models (e.g. GPT-4o) stay below 0.4 — no model saturates.
  • Across all 21 models, no-CoT beats forced-CoT unanimously and significantly: o1-mini 0.696 (no-CoT) vs 0.334 (CoT); DeepSeek-R1 0.757 vs 0.298; Claude-3.5-Sonnet 0.775 vs 0.585.
  • Claude-3.5-Haiku "achieves surprisingly low accuracy" — it frequently misreads the I/O block as "incomplete data" and refuses to answer, a format/comprehension failure independent of task difficulty.
  • RAG-selected shots show no significant difference vs. random selection; models are "quite robust" to erroneous shots even at a 3/4 error rate.

Failure modes

  • Performance saturation/decline beyond ~256–512 shots traced to attention dispersion, confirmed because duplicating one informative shot to reach high nominal counts does not recover lost performance.
  • Models systematically underestimate difficulty when shown ground-truth code: a one-line function rated ~5–7/10 difficulty produced 0% accuracy across all factor-analysis models at all shot counts.
  • SolverLearner (write-code-then-execute) induces rising Do-Not-Finish/Runtime-Error rates with shot count and does not reliably beat standard prompting.

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported, with a title correction already folded in. The corpus previously mis-titled this paper; the verified title is correction "MIR-Bench: Can Your LLM Recognize Complicated Patterns via Many-Shot In-Context Reasoning?" The "demo-count / many-shot lever" framing is directly supported — shot count (4–2048) is the paper's central independent variable.

PCFG SET / Compositionality Decomposed: high task accuracy hides five separate generalization failures

1908.08351
Hupkes, Dankers, Mul, Bruni · JAIR vol. 67, 2020; arXiv v1 2019-08-22, v2 2020-02-23

Claim & novelty

Argues that disagreement over whether neural networks "generalise compositionally" stems from no agreed definition, so the authors collect five theoretically grounded tests — systematicity, productivity, substitutivity, localism, overgeneralisation — and apply them to a string-edit translation task (PCFG SET) across three seq2seq architectures. Central finding: high overall task accuracy does not imply a model satisfies any/all of the five finer-grained criteria. Not an LLM-prompting paper: three architectures (LSTMS2S, ConvS2S, Transformer) are trained from scratch on ~85k labeled pairs, then evaluated on held-out tests.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Each model sees one full input sequence (e.g. repeat A B C) and must output the transformed sequence in one forward decode — no in-context few-shot demonstration channel at all; "few-shot" here means supervised-training-data scarcity, not a prompt-time exemplar set. The operator names (reverse, swap, repeat...) are literal tokens in the input, so no rule induction from demos is required — the model only learns a known symbol's meaning.

Models & headline numbers

  • Task accuracy: LSTMS2S 0.79, ConvS2S 0.85, Transformer 0.92 (best).
  • Systematicity on held-out function-pair recombinations: 0.53/0.56/0.72 — a 33%, 34%, 22% relative drop vs. task accuracy respectively, despite the task being constructed so high accuracy requires systematic recombination.
  • Productivity (≥9-function sequences after training only on ≤8): 0.30/0.31/0.50 — the best architecture still only reaches half.
  • reverse is hard for LSTMS2S/ConvS2S but on par with easy functions for Transformer, because non-sequential attention isn't bound to positional ordering — the one architecture-specific exception the paper flags explicitly.

Failure modes

  • All three architectures fail to generalize to never-co-occurring function pairs, attributed to learning "chunked" representations of frequent pairs rather than separable per-function representations.
  • Localism: unrolling computation constituent-by-constituent changes the model's answer in the majority of cases (consistency only 0.46–0.59) — traced mainly to functions misapplied to strings longer than the training-time max of 5 characters.
  • At very low exception rates (0.01%), LSTMS2S and Transformer never learn the exception, continuing to overgeneralize even after convergence.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The five named generalization axes and the string-edit transduction task are both fully verified. But calling this "the direct template for c23's per-operator independent latent rules" correction overstates the mechanistic similarity: PCFG SET's rules are not latent — the operator name is an explicit input token learned via supervised training, not a hidden rule induced from I/O demos by a frozen model at inference time. The corrected framing: this paper templates the operator taxonomy and independent-axis decomposition style, not the induction-from-demos mechanic that is c23's actual core.

Re-ARC: a procedural generator with no model in the loop, useful only as contamination-proofing precedent

2404.07353
Hodel · arXiv preprint, v1 only, 2024-04-10 · no LLM-evaluation venue (tooling paper)

Claim & novelty

Presents 400 task-specific Python generator+verifier pairs that procedurally produce new, verified input/output examples for each ARC training task by reverse-engineering its assumed example distribution — guaranteeing at least 10,000 unique verified examples per task plus an explicit difficulty-control parameter, versus ARC's native handful of demonstrations. Novelty is positioned against naive data augmentation, which "can't work in general... as the augmentations may break examples"; each new example is instead constructed "from the ground up."

Protocol & observation (exact)

Not applicable — this is a data-generation/verification paper, not an LLM-evaluation paper. It defines no prompting protocol, no model-facing observation format, and reports zero model results. It describes what a generator+verifier pair produces, not what a model observes.

Models & headline numbers

  • No models evaluated — purely a data/tooling contribution.
  • Median generator: 40 lines of code, 22 DSL primitive calls; median verifier: 18 DSL calls; ~1,000 verified unique examples/sec generated for the median task.
  • Difficulty dial: a single interval in [0,1] controlling sampled cardinalities (grid size, object count/size, symbol count); two candidate difficulty metrics (RNG-Difficulty, flagged "heavily flawed"; and PSO-Difficulty) proposed.
  • Verifiers diverge from 11 of 400 original ARC tasks (5 from original bugs/logic changes, 6 with exactly one non-verifying original example).

Failure modes

Generator-level only: some generators have low sample-efficiency (many generated examples are invalid); generation can be slow from small sample spaces or slow verification; the difficulty-interval mechanism is "brittle to some degree" because degrees of freedom are often non-independent (e.g. smaller grids cap feasible object counts), so finely graded difficulty levels can produce empty ranges.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The "procedural ARC generator with a difficulty dial" half is fully verified. But the extracted text contains correction no mention of 1D-ARC anywhere — pairing Re-ARC with 1D-ARC to supply c23's "linearized short-array stratum" is an external design decision made by c23's authors, not something this paper claims or is aware of. Overlap with c23 is family resemblance only: a tunable difficulty dial and an oracle verifier, nothing about prompting protocol or model results.

WILT: strong single-turn reasoners still lose at multi-turn rule discovery, via doom-looping and confirmation bias

2410.10998
Banatt, Cheng, Vaidyanath, Hwu (Riot Games) · arXiv preprint, v1 only, 2024-10-14 · cited elsewhere as submitted to ICLR 2025 (unverified from arXiv metadata alone)

Claim & novelty

Multi-turn inductive reasoning (Wason 2-4-6-inspired): a model must propose test cases over multiple turns, then deduce a hidden boolean rule over three numbers. Best model (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) reaches only 14/50 (28%) on the full split. Models show non-overlapping strengths — hypothesis-space reduction (proposing good tests) vs. function inversion (deducing the rule from evidence). Novelty: memorization-robust by construction (every instance starts from an identical clean-slate prompt with no pre-given examples), plus a decomposition of multi-turn performance into these two measurable sub-skills that prior multi-turn benchmarks don't separately isolate.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Interactive, not single-shot: up to 30 turns of the model proposing a test triple (getting True/False + remaining-attempts feedback), then exactly one final guess as a Python lambda. The model never sees pre-given I/O demonstrations — it must generate its own evidence entirely. A separate single-turn ablation (Sec 4.3) hands the model another model's stripped test cases directly, isolating function-inversion from hypothesis-space-reduction skill.

Models & headline numbers

  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet: 14/50 best on the full split; o1-mini 13/50, o1-preview 12/50 close behind despite far fewer guesses used (avg. 12.1/8.12 vs. Sonnet's 16.38) and near-zero repeats.
  • Lite split (10 easy rules): Claude-3.5-Sonnet 8/10, GPT-4-Turbo 7/10 — "the bulk of the separation... lies in the easier tests."
  • Test-case-swap ablation: o1-mini scores 19/50 when given chatgpt-4o-latest's stripped test cases — higher than any single model's full-task score — showing a composed test-generator+inverter pair beats any single model doing both.
  • Floor: Gemini 1.5 Flash 8b and Gemma 2 9B both 0/50.

Failure modes

  • Doom loop: models (especially smaller ones) repeatedly propose already-tested cases, sometimes dozens of times, with near-identical stated reasoning.
  • Confirmation bias: models fixate on a plausible partial hypothesis and keep proposing confirming rather than falsifying tests, echoing the original Wason 2-4-6 result.
  • o1-mini/o1-preview "underperform due to gathering less evidence... a multi-turn capabilities failure despite not being a failure on any turn in particular."

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. The paper is explicitly Wason-2-4-6-framed, genuinely multi-turn, and explicitly designed to be memorization-robust exactly as claimed. On dating: the corpus's correction of "2025" to Oct 2024 is directly verified (submission 2024-10-14); the "ICLR 2025" venue detail is unverified from the arXiv metadata alone (only v1 exists, no journal-ref).

FalsifyBench: falsification-seeking, not raw capability, predicts success — and no model gets close to optimal

2606.04751
Bertolazzi, Tentori, Bernardi · arXiv preprint, v1 2026-06-03 · no conference/journal venue listed

Claim & novelty

Generalizes Wason 2-4-6 to semantic taxonomies (WordNet): a "player" LLM must discover a hidden general category by iteratively proposing test triples and a hypothesis, receiving Conform/Do-not-conform feedback from a separate "oracle" LLM, until it guesses. Across 12 models, reasoning models are stronger scientific reasoners than instruction-tuned models, but no model approaches optimal performance, and success is driven primarily by falsification-seeking rather than confirmation-seeking behavior. Novelty: a taxonomic sampling design that engineers the hypothesis-too-narrow configuration where negative testing is uniquely optimal, plus turn-level tracking of how each hypothesis relates to the target rule — which the authors say prior Wason-inspired work (including WILT) neglects.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Interactive, not single-shot: up to 20 player-oracle rounds; the player is stateful (its history accumulates), the oracle is stateless (freshly initialized each call). The player sees only 3 initial example items plus the system prompt — it is never shown the target rule or sampling category directly. Output per turn is a structured JSON action (test triple + hypothesis + reasoning, or a guess), not a single short string. The verifier is itself an LLM oracle, validated against human annotation only for a 1,200-instance sample.

Models & headline numbers

  • Best instruction-tuned model DeepSeek-V3.1: 41% success vs. top reasoning models GPT-5.2-Chat 75% and GPT-OSS-120B 68%; remaining instruction-tuned models cluster 33–35%.
  • Small reasoning models underperform: GPT-5-Nano 12%, Qwen3.5-9B 21% — worse than all instruction-tuned models.
  • Confirmation bias vs. success rate: Spearman ρ = −0.779 (p=0.003); vs. conclusive falsification rate: ρ = −0.937 (p<0.001).
  • Oracle quality only weakly predicts player failure: oracle-error regression coefficient is near-zero and not credible, while confirmation-bias coefficient is robustly negative (OR 0.016).
  • "Artifact" is uniformly the hardest target category (0–30% success for most models); "animal" is easiest (GPT-5.2-Chat reaches 100%).

Failure modes

  • Confirmation bias / positive-testing dominance: instruction-tuned models cluster at 66–82% confirmation bias vs. 24–39% for reasoning models.
  • Surface-level linguistic hypotheses (e.g. "all items start with letter a"): systematically associated with failure for stronger models (GLM-5 97.6%, GPT-5.2-Chat 92.0% of failed games), but weak models show no such pattern — suggesting they fail for more basic reasons.
  • Llama-4-Maverick relies on "repeated guessing rather than active falsification" (avg. 6.92 guesses/game, 81.8% confirmation bias).

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. The paper is explicitly Wason-2-4-6-inspired, unambiguously multi-turn, and its own Related Work section places itself alongside WILT as a closely related benchmark — directly substantiating the "overlapping the WILT... cluster" framing. The paper does not itself mention HERO'S JOURNEY; that grouping is a corpus-side clustering judgment, not something FalsifyBench asserts about itself — a minor scope note, not a miscitation.

c23's target cell is genuinely empty, and empty partly because it is hard to keep reachable

The crowded regions are "state the latent rule as a symbolic artifact" (IB, WILT, Falsify — the well-populated evaluation mode, paid for with expensive or noisy verifiers) and multi-turn Wason-style rule discovery (WILT and Falsify are near-duplicates on two of three axes). c23's target cell — single-call × apply-rule-to-held-out-query-with-exact-match × latent composed K-independent rules with a partial-credit ladder — is near-empty: MIR is the closest neighbor (single-call, apply-and-exact-match) but its functions are monolithic with no partial-rule-credit structure; IB shares the subregular substrate but grades rule-set reconstruction against the training sample, not a held-out query. No dossier paper occupies c23's exact cell. The load-bearing caveat spanning the whole map: IB's 0.00–5.69% compatibility finding is a documented reachability warning for anything built on the subregular substrate c23 reuses — the empty cell is empty partly because it is hard to keep the ceiling reachable there.

Capability synthesis

One-shot succeeds only at the easy end; multistep collapses via evidence overload and confirmation bias, not budget exhaustion

Single forward-pass induction is tractable only in the easy, single-rule, small-k regime InductionBench (IB) tests: ISL at k=2/vocab=2/rules=1 yields 40–60% compatibility for GPT-4o/Llama-3.3-70B, but at k=4/rules=3 all but o3-mini collapse to 0.00. c23's reseed-only, single-rule slice deliberately restricts k, so it lives in IB's partial-success regime, not the zero-collapse regime — but the reachable ceiling is genuinely narrow, not near-100%. Context window k is the dominant difficulty driver, and IB shows something structurally important: adding new demonstration data can make performance worse, not better ("more evidence can hurt"), while MIR shows many-shot performance simply saturates by ~256 shots without declining as sharply. In multi-turn settings (WILT, FalsifyBench) the dominant failure is structurally different — doom-looping and confirmation bias, not running out of turns — and WILT's own ablation shows composing a separate evidence-gatherer with a separate inverter (o1-mini given another model's tests: 19/50) beats any single model doing both (best full-task score: 14/50). Both failure modes are structurally impossible in c23's single-shot design: there is no adaptive evidence-gathering to doom-loop on, and demos are fixed rather than self-generated.

Cheap-tier models carry an extra format-refusal risk layered on top of the difficulty curve

No dossier tests c23's exact cheap tier (Gemini-2.5-Flash / GPT-5-mini / Claude Haiku 4.5 / DeepSeek-Chat), but same-family proxies converge on two distinct risk sources. First, the difficulty curve itself: GPT-4o and DeepSeek-V3-class models hit 0.00 compatibility at IB's moderate setting but 40–60% at the easy end, so difficulty curation matters enormously. Second, a separate comprehension/format failure layered on top: MIR reports Claude-3.5-Haiku "often does not understand our prompt and sees the target input as part of an incomplete data, thus refusing to answer," and WILT independently shows Claude 3 Haiku at 1/50 and Gemini-1.5-Flash-8b at 0/50, both flagged for failing to use available turns/effort rather than reasoning incorrectly. This is a parsing/refusal risk, not a reasoning-difficulty risk — it can drag cheap-tier scores to near-zero independent of how easy the underlying rule is, and it is fixable by prompt-format work rather than task curation. MIR additionally shows no-CoT beats forced-CoT unanimously across 21 models (e.g. +0.36 for o1-mini), predicting that direct-answer prompting, not CoT, is the right default for c23's non-thinking cheap tier.

The InductionBench-shaped baseline is easier to score than to solve: held-out exact-match should modestly outperform rule-reconstruction

c23's format — demos plus one held-out query, exact-match on the transformed string — is easier to succeed at than InductionBench's own protocol, which grades a full rule-set reconstruction via precision/recall/compatibility against the training sample. A model can sometimes transform one held-out string correctly without articulating the complete minimal rule set, so IB's compatibility numbers are a conservative-to-pessimistic proxy for c23's easier task — the single largest source of upward uncertainty in the floor/ceiling estimate below. Countervailing this: IB's "more demos can hurt" and "few-shot negligible past a complexity threshold" findings mean prompt-side levers have limited headroom to push past that ceiling once difficulty is fixed.

Predicted floor and ceiling for the c23 baseline

c23 config: InductionBench-style subregular ISL/OSL, fresh seeds, single-rule instances only (no composition ladder), existing I/O-demos-plus-query format, short-string exact match, cheap tier, temp 0. This is the intersection of IB's partial-success regime (single rule, small k) and a held-out-query protocol that should modestly outrank IB's own rule-reconstruction metric — but no paper tests this exact cell, so both ranges carry real uncertainty, skewed toward the low end by format-refusal risk on the cheapest models.

Uncertainty is high and skewed low for the floor: MIR's Claude-Haiku refusal behavior and WILT's Haiku/Flash-8b near-zero scores show cheap models can score near zero purely from misreading the I/O block, but single-rule small-k ISL is genuinely partially solvable, and held-out-query exact-match is easier than full-rule reconstruction, pulling above zero. Go/no-go gate: the predicted ~20–40 point floor-to-ceiling gap is usable IF difficulty is curated (small k/vocab, small demo count, direct-answer prompting) and format-refusal is fixed first; if reseed-only inherits IB's default hardness, the band collapses toward zero and the task needs added easing, not added complexity, before proceeding — calibrate on a pilot before freezing, as both IB and MIR did (MIR excluded all-zero instances from its retained pool; IB explicitly dialed k/|Σ|/rules).

Positioning & publishability synthesis

Highest overlap is InductionBench; the phase-1 baseline is close to a re-scored IB instance

c23 literally vendors IB's generator+oracle. What differs: IB asks the model to state a full symbolic rule set graded against the training sample; c23 asks the model to apply the rule to a held-out query, scored by cheap string exact-match. c23 adds a multi-family ladder (casing, swaps, command→action, 1D-ARC) and reachability curation — directly answering IB's own 0–5.69% ceiling warning. Honest overlap: the un-optimized baseline as planned is very close to an IB instance re-scored under exact-match-on-a-query instead of compatibility; the novelty of the baseline phase is the scoring/format change and fresh seeds, not a new task substrate. The genuinely new part — the composition ladder — is explicitly deferred out of phase 1.

Most threatening overlap is MIR-Bench — it already owns the "single-call, apply, exact-match" mode

c23 risks being seen as "MIR-Bench with a smaller string domain and a rule-count ladder." The differentiator that must hold: MIR's functions are monolithic with "no equivalent partial-rule-credit structure," while c23's decomposable K-independent-rule structure resolves which rule strata a model misses, not just pass/fail. MIR's own findings are direct confounds c23 must control for: no-CoT beats forced-CoT unanimously; RAG-selected shots don't beat random; performance saturates by ~256 shots (not relevant at c23's small demo counts, but a reminder that "more demos" is not a free lever in this family generally).

WILT and FalsifyBench are the cleanest complementarity story: c23 isolates the sub-capability their own interaction loops confound

c23 sits in the opposite protocol quadrant from WILT and FalsifyBench (single-call vs. interactive). Both interactive papers show that success is dominated by strategy (evidence-gathering, falsification-seeking) rather than by raw per-turn reasoning correctness — WILT's o1-mini/o1-preview "underperform due to gathering less evidence... a multi-turn capabilities failure despite not being a failure on any turn in particular," and FalsifyBench's confirmation-bias-vs-success correlation (ρ=−0.779) dwarfs any raw-capability signal. c23's single-call design directly tests whether the same underlying induction capability holds up when the interaction loop and its confounds (doom-looping, confirmation bias, turn-budget management) are removed entirely — a question neither paper's own design can answer about itself.

If optimization yields large, verified gains, the precise claim is an optimizer-driven delta, not a capability claim

The defensible workshop claim: automated prompt optimization (COPRO/MIPROv2/GEPA) raises a fresh-seed, single-rule, contamination-proof induction baseline by a measured Δ, verified (i) held-out-seed generalizing, (ii) not attributable to demo-selection alone (MIR shows RAG selection doesn't beat random), and (iii) not a CoT-toggling artifact (MIR shows no-CoT unanimously beats forced-CoT). What it must not say: that LLMs "can/cannot do inductive reasoning" — IB already owns the negative capability claim at this substrate, and one task cannot support a positive general claim; nor that the composition ladder was tested — it is deferred to phase 2's task design, not the baseline.

Lead with prompt-optimizer benchmarking, not induction-reasoning evaluation — and know the early-kill gate

Induction-family evaluation is a thin line here: IB already owns the "even the simplest complexity class fails" finding, and a single-rule, single-task result is thin without the composition ladder. The stronger line is prompt-optimizer benchmarking (GEPA is an ICLR 2026 oral, beating MIPROv2 by ~13% aggregate at ~35× fewer rollouts; MAS-PromptBench, 2026, is a benchmark specifically for comparing optimizers) — a clean, cheap, deterministically-verified task with decomposable-in-principle structure is exactly what that line wants. Not publishable: no gain / within-noise gains, gains that vanish on fresh seeds or reduce to demo-selection/CoT-toggling, an unreachable-ceiling task (IB's 0–5% regime), or a bare IB re-run with no optimizer story and no ladder.
