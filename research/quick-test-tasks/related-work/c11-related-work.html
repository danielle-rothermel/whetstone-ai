c11 Related Work: House-Convention Canonicalizer

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-22

No paper in this 7-paper corpus runs c11's exact task — canonical-JSON serialization scored by whole-string exact match against a deterministic oracle, under invented, standard-deviating rules. Every dossier says so explicitly. The closest structural cousins score with partial-credit verifiers (F1, WER, BLEU, keyword-match, schema-validity), so every number below is an adjacent-task proxy, not a direct measurement. IFBench is the most dangerous neighbor (same verifiable-stratified-contamination-proof paradigm, even overlapping CSV/date constraints); the field otherwise clusters in a graded-verifier, public-or-real-convention, full-object-or-free-text region that c11's design deliberately sits outside of.

Paper dossiers

EDC: canonicalization framing overlaps, but it targets semantic equivalence classes, not syntactic house rules

2404.03868
Zhang & Soh (National University of Singapore) · EMNLP 2024

Claim & novelty

Extract-Define-Canonicalize (EDC) is a three-phase pipeline for knowledge-graph construction: open information extraction, then LLM-generated schema definitions, then post-hoc canonicalization of relation phrases into one canonical label — without needing the full target schema in-prompt. An optional Refinement phase (EDC+R) adds a trained Schema Retriever (fine-tuned E5-mistral-7b-instruct). Headline: EDC beats specialized trained baselines (REGEN, GenIE) and a clustering canonicalizer (CESI) across three datasets, without any parameter tuning of the base LLM.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Multi-call pipeline, not single-shot: each of the three phases is a separate few-shot prompt; Refinement re-runs the pipeline, feeding back the model's own prior extractions plus retriever-suggested schema components as a "hint." No externally supplied oracle feedback between iterations — refinement is self-bootstrapped. Canonicalization targets semantic-equivalence classes (e.g. "profession"/"job"/"occupation" → one meaning-label), verified by LLM judgment and embedding retrieval, not a fixed syntactic rule set.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4 (best OIE model): WebNLG Partial F1 0.783 (EDC) → 0.820 (EDC+R), beating REGEN's 0.767; REBEL Partial F1 0.546 → 0.601 vs GenIE's 0.385; Wiki-NRE 0.683 → 0.713 vs GenIE's 0.484.
  • Schema Retriever ablation (GPT-3.5-turbo): removing it drops WebNLG Partial F1 0.794→0.752, REBEL 0.559→0.517 — "a notable decline."
  • A second refinement pass (EDC+2xR) yields near-zero additional gain (WebNLG 0.794→0.797) — diminishing returns.
  • Mistral-7b benefits less from the refinement hint than GPT-3.5/GPT-4 — "not as able to leverage the provided hints," directly relevant to the cheap tier.

Failure modes

The paper's main reported "failure" is scorer/dataset noise, not model error: EDC is penalized for producing semantically-correct triplets absent from non-exhaustive REBEL/Wiki-NRE reference sets. CESI (the clustering baseline) over-generalizes, conflating "place of death," "place of birth," "date of death," "date of birth" into one category.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. EDC does center a canonicalize step and anchors the shared vocabulary/framing. But the corpus description of EDC as "primarily c12's parent" is the load-bearing qualifier: EDC's target is semantic equivalence (many phrasings → one meaning), not syntactic/formatting convention (key order, quoting, number format) the way c11's house rules are, and the paper never mentions JSON canonicalization, RFC 8785/JCS, or slugification at all. The connection is real but only at the level of shared high-level framing.

JSONSchemaBench: c11's schema-pool donor grades structural complexity, not canonicalization difficulty — and its "contamination-proofed" framing is unsupported

2501.10868
Geng, Cooper, Moskal, Jenkins, Berman, Ranchin, West, Horvitz, Nori (EPFL / Microsoft / JSON Schema) · arXiv preprint, v3 2025-02-27, not noted as peer-reviewed

Claim & novelty

Introduces a ~9,558-schema real-world benchmark (after filtering) and a three-axis evaluation (efficiency, coverage, quality) across six constrained-decoding frameworks (Guidance, Outlines, Llamacpp, XGrammar, OpenAI, Gemini). Headline: constrained decoding speeds generation up to 50%, the best framework covers ~2x as many real-world schemas as the worst, and constrained decoding improves downstream accuracy by up to ~4% even on minimally-structured tasks like GSM8K.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot, greedy/zero-temperature, one generation per instance. Coverage: "a simple instruction with two-shot examples," validated against the schema by the jsonschema library. Quality: a CoT-eliciting prompt requiring output {"reasoning": ..., "answer": ...} in one generation. Schemas are graded into five complexity tiers by field count (trivial/small/medium/large/ultra), drawn from GitHub, JSON Schema Store, GlaiveAI, Kubernetes, Snowplow, and Washington Post ANS.

Models & headline numbers

  • Guidance wins empirical coverage on 6 of 8 datasets; achieves full JSON Schema Test Suite coverage on 13 of 43 categories vs. 1 each for Llamacpp/XGrammar and 0 for Outlines.
  • Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (GSM8K quality task): LM-only 80.1% → Guidance 83.8%, "~3% improvement over the LM-only approach in every task."
  • Closed-source engines (OpenAI/Gemini) have the lowest empirical coverage but highest compliance rates — "a more conservative strategy."
  • LM-only (no constrained decoding) has the lowest compliance rate overall — "unreliability as a standalone solution."

Failure modes

Over-constrained (rejects valid instances) vs. under-constrained (allows invalid ones); Outlines times out (40s–10min) on minItems/maxItems/enum/array features. A hypothesized quality-degrading mechanism: constrained decoding can push a model off-distribution (blocking the comma in "89,000" could force "890000" instead of "89000") — directly relevant to c11's number-canonicalization sub-rule.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported, with a correction. The "graded real-world schema corpus" half holds — five complexity tiers by field count, from real sources. But correction: "contamination-proofed by regeneration" is unsupported. The paper describes a static, one-time collection (Sec. 3.1/Appendix A.1); the words "contamination" and "regenerat*" never appear. The released static schema set is, if anything, a contamination risk for future training, not a proofing mechanism — c11 must supply its own regeneration step (e.g. via json-schema-faker) to get that property.

StructEval: JSON generation is saturated for strong models, but rare formats (TOML) collapse to single digits — a floor prior for c11's harder strata

2505.20139
Yang, Jiang, He, Siu, et al. (20 authors, U. Waterloo) · arXiv preprint, v3 2026-04-02, no peer-reviewed venue listed

Claim & novelty

LLMs are systematically worse at structured-output generation than fluent free text, and the gap is uneven: worse for generation-from-scratch than format-to-format conversion, worse for rendered/visual formats than text-only ones, and worse for rare formats (TOML, Mermaid, TikZ) than common ones (JSON, HTML). Even the best model (GPT-4o) averages only 76.02% across 44 tasks and 18 formats; best open model (Qwen3-4B) trails by ~10 points.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Zero-shot, single-shot, one uniform prompt template, no interactive feedback loop. The model sees a natural-language task description plus itemized feature requirements (or a structured input to convert), wrapped in a fixed instruction template requiring <|BEGIN_CODE|>/<|END_CODE|> delimiters and no other text. No few-shot demos, no CoT instruction, no per-turn feedback.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4o: highest overall average 76.02% (T-gen 91.52, T-conv 73.95, V-gen 65.39, V-conv 73.20).
  • o1-mini (reasoning): 75.58 average — no decisive edge over GPT-4o despite reasoning.
  • Qwen3-4B (best open-source): 67.04 average, trailing GPT-4o by ~10 points.
  • Commercial models average 75.78% on T-gen under temp=1, vs. open models averaging just 8.58% specifically on TOML generation in that same set; Phi-3-mini scores 0.00 on T→TOML.
  • JSON/CSV/YAML generation is "saturated" >90–100% for strong models (GPT-4o T→JSON 99.36, T→CSV 100).

Failure modes

Worst model (Phi-3-mini-128k-instruct) emits a malformed closing delimiter (|<|END_CODE|>) on TOML→YAML, and loses hierarchical parent-child structure on CSV→JSON. Rare/visual formats (Mermaid, TikZ, Vega) collapse below 0.5 for essentially all models, attributed to advanced visual reasoning being concentrated in multimodal commercial LLMs.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported, with a count correction. The non-renderable (JSON/YAML/CSV/TOML) slice and below-ceiling floor evidence both hold. correction: the corpus flagged an internal count discrepancy (abstract says 18 formats, introduction says 21) — four separate body-text passages converge on 18 as the paper's intended figure; "21" in the introduction is the authors' own uncorrected inconsistency, not an extraction error.

IFBench: c11's closest and most dangerous neighbor — novel standard-deviating constraints crater frontier models below 50%

2507.02833
Pyatkin, Malik, Graf, Ivison, Huang, Dasigi, Lambert, Hajishirzi (AI2 / U. Washington) · NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks Track

Claim & novelty

Most SOTA models strongly overfit to IFEval's fixed 25 verifiable constraints and fail to generalize to unseen ones. IFBench: 300 prompts, 58 new out-of-domain verifiable constraints (7 categories), on which GPT-4.1, Claude 3.7/4 Sonnet, and Qwen3-32B all score below 50%. RLVR training (IF-RLVR, via GRPO) substantially closes the gap in-domain and improves — but does not saturate — out-of-domain generalization.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Both single-turn and multi-turn, but both are single forward-pass-per-turn generations scored by an automatic Python verifier per constraint — no interactive correction loop. Single-turn: one user prompt concatenates the task with 1–2 constraints (e.g. "Use at least N different coordinating conjunctions"). Multi-turn: task → a prior assistant response r1 → a third turn asking to rewrite r1 to satisfy constraint c, scored only on the final output; no scored intermediate feedback.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4.1, Claude 3.7/4 Sonnet, Qwen3-32B: all score below 50% on IFBench despite 80+% on IFEval.
  • IF-RLVR raises Tulu-3-8B: IFEval 82.4→92.2, IFBench 28.9→44.6/45.9 (minor cross-table discrepancy in the paper itself).
  • IF-RLVR raises a Qwen2.5-7B base model to IFEval 87.8 / IFBench 53.7–54.7.
  • o3 remains the only out-of-the-box model IF-RLVR-trained models can't beat.
  • GRPO strictly dominates DPO at matched data/prompts (IFEval 89.65 vs 79.67, IFBench 30.6 vs 29.3).

Failure modes

Overfitting to a fixed constraint template set; reward hacking (IF-RLVR models "prioritize the constraint" over the underlying task); range-generalization failure when train/test variable ranges are disjoint; failure is correlated across models on the same hard instances, not idiosyncratic.

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. The "58 new verifiable constraints... programmatic exact checks" and "out-of-domain" framing are both verbatim-confirmed. IFBench's "custom" category even contains CSV-quoting/delimiter and date-reformat constraints that structurally overlap c11's instance families — the sharpest overlap in the corpus. The decisive difference: IFBench verifies a property of an otherwise open-ended free-text response (many strings pass), while c11's ceiling is one specific reference string with whole-match 0/1.

StructText: facts survive table-to-text narrativization near-ceiling, but re-extracting them back to structure collapses — a design lesson, not a task overlap

2507.21340
Kashyap, Shirai, Mihindukulasooriya, Samulowitz (IBM Research) · VLDB 2025 Workshop (TaDA), v1 only

Claim & novelty

An automated "plan-then-execute" pipeline turns tabular data into synthetic natural-language text, then uses the (text, ground-truth-table) pair as a contamination-resistant benchmark source for text-to-table extraction. Central dichotomy: LLMs generating the synthetic text achieve near-ceiling factuality/hallucination-avoidance and numeric/temporal fidelity, but the resulting prose has low coherence and is hard for an LLM-based extractor to parse back into structured key-value pairs.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot for both directions, no iterative repair loop. Generation: model receives ~10 sample rows plus a planning prompt, groups columns into 1–5 report types, then in a second single-shot call receives a semicolon-delimited key-value string (e.g. revenue: 2.65595E+11; revenue_period: 9/29/18) and must produce one coherent paragraph. Extraction: model predicts a column schema, then returns one JSON dict of key-value extractions per row.

Models & headline numbers

  • Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct (generation): factuality 4.58/4.56, hallucination 4.90/4.55, but coherence only 3.28/3.53 (SEC/WikiDB, 1–5 scale) — coherence is the clear weak dimension.
  • Numeric/temporal fidelity: all four P/R/F1 triples exceed 0.9 F1 (SEC numeric F1 0.927, WikiDB temporal F1 0.915).
  • Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (baseline extraction): Column Identification F1 only 0.455 (SEC) / 0.413 (WikiDB); Value Extraction precision as low as 0.11–0.26.

Failure modes

A structured-to-prose-to-structured information-accessibility gap: facts are preserved with high fidelity in prose, but that same prose resists automated re-structuring. A secondary parsing-ambiguity case: the model correctly extracts "fiscal year 2024" as a valid temporal value absent from ground truth, deflating precision for reasons that are not a real model error.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The synthetic table-to-text generator description is accurate (71,539 examples / 49 datasets from SEC filings and WikiDB). correction: the paper never proposes or evaluates a canonicalization task — its entire apparatus is fuzzy, tolerant scoring (LLM-judge rubrics, Levenshtein/BERTScore, 0.1% numeric tolerance), and the word "canonical" appears only incidentally. "Reversible into a canonicalization instance source" is the dossier's own plausible extrapolation, not a claim the paper makes.

PolyNorm: real-locale normalization is a live few-shot win over rule-based systems, but its conventions are recallable and its verifier is soft

2511.03080
Wong, Alshehri, Kao, He (Apple) · arXiv preprint v1, 2025-11-05; comments field cites EMNLP 2025 Industry Track

Claim & novelty

Few-shot LLM text normalization (PolyNorm) can replace rule-based (WFST) TN systems, achieving consistent WER reductions across 8 typologically diverse languages against Apple Siri's production rule-based baseline, with far less engineering effort. Also contributes PolyNorm-Benchmark, a language-agnostic automatic data-curation/evaluation pipeline.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot per instance: a structured 3-part prompt (Instruction Prompt + 80–100 curated ICL examples per language + target input), model outputs one normalized string per call. No per-instance interactive loop — but the researchers themselves ran an offline "Error Analysis and Hillclimbing Iteration" across development to refine the ICL example set between benchmark runs (author-side curation, not model-side interaction).

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4o: WER 4.17%–7.88% across all 8 languages, vs. baseline WER 9.72%–17.49%.
  • GPT-4o-mini: WER 6.60%–11.19% — worse than baseline only for Lithuanian (10.54 vs 10.04).
  • Iterative ICL-curation effect: Japanese WER dropped from 12.32% to 7.88%, Lithuanian from 12.22% to 6.99%, purely from refining which examples are shown.

Failure modes

Incorrect numeral expansions and inconsistent date/currency-format handling. Baseline (rule-based) failures include a German compound-number segmentation error and a French sports-score dash-convention error. Limitations note performance is "highly dependent on the quality and representativeness" of the ICL examples.

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported, with a clarification already present in the c11 framing. The paper does show few-shot LLMs beat a rule-based baseline on date/phone/currency/cardinal/ordinal normalization. The needed clarification: PolyNorm's targets are real, recallable locale conventions, not invented standard-deviating rules, and its verifier is soft (WER/BLEU/CER) rather than exact-match — so it is evidence for general feasibility, not for c11's specific stress point.

LLMStructBench: schema compliance saturates with scale, but wrong-value errors never go away — and this paper is not actually cited on c11's page

2602.14743
Tenckhoff, Koddenbrock, Rodner (HTW Berlin) · arXiv preprint, v1 only, submitted 2026-02-16, no venue on record

Claim & novelty

A 995-instance, five-use-case benchmark of NL-message-to-JSON extraction, scored by F1_micro (fuzzy, coercion-aware value/token accuracy) plus DOC_micro (document-level full validity). Across 22 open-weight models (0.6B–70B) plus GPT-4o and five prompting strategies, the paper's conclusion is that prompting strategy matters more than model scale for schema-compliant, semantically correct JSON generation.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one forward call per model per strategy. Model receives the NL message, one example (text, reference-JSON) pair, and the target JSON schema; returns a single JSON object in one shot. The five strategies (J/P/PJ/J+/PJ+) vary only how schema/format guidance is delivered (API format param vs. in-prompt schema+example) — none are multi-turn.

Models & headline numbers

  • F1_micro is flat and high, 0.89–0.96 across all 22 models 0.6B–70B; DOC_micro is volatile, 0.01–0.52.
  • GPT-4o and Gemma3-27B tie at the top: F1_micro 0.96, DOC_micro 0.52, composite 0.74 — GPT-4o "did not exhibit a clear advantage over the best open-source models."
  • Worst: Phi3-3.8B at F1_micro 0.95 but DOC_micro just 0.01 under strategy P.
  • Strategy win-count across 22 models: P wins 11, PJ+ wins 8, PJ wins 3, J and J+ win 0.
  • Wrong-Value (WV) errors dominate 96–100% of remaining errors even for the best models.

Failure modes

Missing-Key/schema-violation failures concentrate in small models under weak prompting (Deepseek-R1-1.5B: 36.8% MK under P) and vanish by ~7–8B or under stronger schema-enforcing prompts. Once structural failures are eliminated, Wrong-Value becomes the persistent bottleneck at every scale. Switching prompting strategy can trade one failure mode for another rather than eliminating it — eliminating MK errors "redirects almost every remaining uncertainty into field content."

Corpus-claim verdict

Miscited — flag this prominently. The corpus framing implies this paper is cited on c11's page; it is not. c11.md's only mention of it is a closing provenance-flag footnote stating it is a "provenance-unverified, future-dated corpus item attributed to c12, not c11" — correction: this paper is substantively more relevant to c12 (schema-conformant extraction) than to c11 (canonicalization under invented house rules). Its identity itself is genuinely verified (real paper, real authors, real arXiv id) — only its attribution to c11 is the error.

Positioning: three axes discriminate this family

Single-shot-vs-multi-turn turns out to be nearly degenerate for this family — EDC's "multi-turn" is self-bootstrapped refinement and IFBench's is constraint-isolation across a fixed 3-turn context, neither is an oracle-in-the-loop correction protocol, and every other paper is single-shot at evaluation time. The three axes that actually separate the set: verifier granularity (exact whole-string 0/1 → per-field/constraint boolean → graded partial credit → soft edit-distance/LLM-judge), convention provenance (invented adversarially-deviating rules → public-standard conformance → real/recallable convention → emergent/model-chosen), and output shape (single canonical string vs. full structured object vs. free NL text).

Paper / task Verifier granularity Convention provenance Output shape
c11 (as designed) Exact whole-string 0/1, no partial credit Invented, adversarially deviating from RFC 8785 JCS / ISO / slugify Single canonical string
PolyNorm Soft: WER / BLEU / CER, partial credit Real locale conventions (recallable) Single normalized string
IFBench Per-constraint programmatic boolean (strict/loose) Invented-but-instructed, restated per instance Free NL text satisfying constraints
JSONSchemaBench Schema validator; broad valid-class Public JSON-Schema standard Full JSON instance (any valid one)
StructEval (T-slice) Graded: 0.2·syntax + 0.8·keyword/dot-path (+VQA for V) Public formats (JSON/YAML/CSV/TOML) Full structured document
LLMStructBench Graded fuzzy: Levenshtein/coercion F1_micro + DOC_micro Public JSON schema (no house rules) Full nested JSON object (extraction)
StructText Fuzzy: LLM-judge rubric + BERTScore/Levenshtein, 0.1% numeric tol. Emergent, model-chosen groupings Free NL prose (gen) / JSON dict (extract)
EDC Mixed: token-triplet Partial/Strict/Exact F1 + human eval Semantic-equivalence classes, discovered/retrieved schema Set of (subj, rel, obj) triplets

c11 occupies an empty cell: no paper combines exact whole-string match with invented adversarial conventions on a single-string output

Six of seven papers cluster in the graded/fuzzy/soft verifier band; IFBench is the only other hard per-item boolean, and even it offers strict-vs-loose leniency. On convention provenance, the field splits between public-standard conformance (JSONSchemaBench, StructEval, LLMStructBench) and real/recallable convention (PolyNorm, StructText, EDC); no paper occupies c11's cell of invented conventions that adversarially deviate from a named public standard and stay stable across instances rather than being restated per prompt. That emptiness is c11's differentiation and its publishability risk at once: the cell can be empty because it is unclaimed, or because it is a narrow instrument the field does not need as a benchmark in its own right.

Capability synthesis

One-shot: JSON structure is nearly solved, full-document exact correctness tops out near 0.52 even for GPT-4o; no paper tests an oracle-in-the-loop repair protocol

Structural JSON validity in one pass is nearly saturated for familiar formats (LLMStructBench's F1_micro 0.89–0.96 flat across 0.6B–70B; StructEval's JSON/CSV/YAML generation >90–100% for strong models). But full-document exact correctness tops out at DOC_micro ~0.52 even for GPT-4o/Gemma3-27B (LLMStructBench), and novel, standard-deviating constraints crater one-shot accuracy below 50% even for frontier models (IFBench) — the single most c11-relevant SHOWN result, since c11's house rules function like that same unfamiliar-convention axis. No paper tests a per-instance interactive repair loop with oracle feedback — EDC's "multi-turn" is self-bootstrapped refinement that helps once then saturates fast (near-zero gain on a second pass), and weaker models (Mistral-7b) exploit added context less than GPT-3.5/GPT-4, directly relevant to c11's cheap tier.

Cheap-tier: high per-field accuracy but low full-document validity; prompting strategy dominates model scale, and few-shot curation is a large, double-edged lever

The cheap-tier proxies (0.6–14B open models, "-mini" closed models) show high per-field accuracy but low whole-document validity: even 0.6–1.7B models reach F1_micro 0.92–0.94 but DOC_micro only 0.10–0.40. Prompting strategy beats model scale at this tier (LLMStructBench: DOC_micro swings 0.01→0.52 from strategy choice alone). GPT-4o-mini/GPT-4.1-mini trail their flagship siblings by only ~3 points on StructEval but are meaningfully worse on PolyNorm's WER. Format collapse is worst at the cheap/open tier (StructEval: open models score 0.00–16 on TOML vs. commercial 75.78% average). Curated few-shot examples are a large lever (PolyNorm: 4–5 point WER swings from ICL curation alone) but table/demo coverage is a leakage risk c11's own design already flags.

Serialization-convention ablation — c11's exact axis — has zero measured effect size anywhere in this corpus

No paper does a controlled ablation of key order, quoting, whitespace, or number format — the exact axis c11 varies. Every dossier's representation-format field states this explicitly. The nearest lever with a measured effect is in-prompt schema + one example (LLMStructBench: DOC_micro 0.01→0.52 from strategy alone) and curated few-shot ICL sets (PolyNorm: 4–5 point WER swings). Constrained decoding helps compliance (JSONSchemaBench: +3% on every quality task) but is a decoding-engine intervention unavailable to c11's plain prompt-only baseline, and the evidence conflicts on whether tighter output constraints help or hurt — JSON Schema's canonical-output framing helped, but StructEval/LLMStructBench show forced structure can introduce new errors.

Predicted floor and ceiling for c11's plain-JCS baseline

c11's actual first run is plain RFC 8785 (JCS) canonical-JSON — no invented house-rule deviations yet — inputs from pinned json-schema-faker schemas, oracle = trailofbits rfc8785-py, exact match, cheap tier, temp 0. No paper measures exact-match canonical JSON directly, so both ranges below are inference built on adjacent proxies, with real uncertainty in both directions.

Naive floor (best guess ~15%)
~5–30%
Ceiling, best prompt (best guess ~55%)
~40–75%

Reasoning: the model will reliably emit valid JSON (a saturated, familiar structural capability), but plain JCS requires simultaneously getting UTF-16 key sort order, ECMA-shortest-round-trip number formatting, exact escaping, and zero insignificant whitespace all correct in one string for a 0/1 score — the number-canonicalization sub-rule in particular is unlikely to reach 100% even with worked examples; it is the c11 analog of the Wrong-Value bottleneck that "does not go away with scale or prompting" (LLMStructBench). Uncertainty is high: no paper tests this exact cell.

Conflict to surface, not resolve: IFBench's novel-constraint evidence argues for a low floor (JCS rules are convention-following the cheap tier is bad at). But plain JCS is a published, real standard — JSONSchemaBench/StructEval both show JSON structure is the saturated, familiar format everywhere in this corpus. If cheap models have absorbed JCS behavior from training data (plausible — the spec and rfc8785 libraries are public), the plain-JCS floor could be substantially higher than 15%, possibly 40%+. This is exactly why the candidate design flags that the plain-JCS variant has no invented deviations yet: the go/no-go hinges on whether plain JCS is already too easy (memorizable) to be discriminative. Recommendation: run the plain-JCS baseline first and check the naive floor specifically on number-heavy / unicode-heavy schemas, since the corpus consistently identifies number formatting as the break point.

Positioning & publishability synthesis

IFBench already owns "verifiable, decomposable, contamination-proof constraint-following" — c11's differentiation must be argued precisely, not assumed

Overlap with IFBench is deep and mechanical: both are programmatically-verifiable, reference-function-as-ceiling, stratified-by-rule tasks with per-rule failure attribution, and IFBench's own "custom" category contains CSV-quoting and date-reformat constraints that structurally overlap c11's instances. The decisive differences rest narrowly on two axes: (1) IFBench verifies a property of an open-ended response (many surface strings pass); c11's ceiling is one specific reference string, whole-match 0/1; (2) IFBench's rules are stated in natural language per instance; c11's are a fixed latent convention held across held-out instances. Because IFBench already owns the family paradigm, c11 cannot claim the paradigm as new — only the axis-level refinement.

The plain-JCS baseline sits in the crowded, recallable-standard cell — a large gain there is a materially weaker result than a gain on the invented-rule task

Plain JCS canonicalization is public, documented, and recallable — it sits in the crowded "public-standard-conformance" region alongside JSONSchemaBench/StructEval/LLMStructBench, not in c11's differentiating "invented-adversarial" cell. A large verified optimizer gain on the plain-JCS-only baseline is a result about prompt-optimizing a model to emit a known public canonical form — contestable and non-novel. The stronger, more novel claim requires the full invented-house-rule task (post-diet, table-width > minibatch/demo width) with a per-rule breakdown showing the gain concentrates in learnable rule types while computation-heavy strata sit at a known ceiling.

A large verified gain belongs in prompt-optimizer benchmarking (Area 9), not the family's own saturated evaluation line

The family's own evaluation line (verifiable instruction-following / structured-output compliance) is dense: IFBench and JSONSchemaBench already cover it. The novel object c11 can contribute is not "a new capability benchmark" but "a clean, exact-match, contamination-proof, per-rule-decomposable task on which optimizer O beats optimizer O′ by Δ" — riding GEPA's ICLR 2026 oral and MAS-PromptBench's 2026 optimizer-comparison momentum. Reviewers will demand a full COPRO/MIPROv2/GEPA sweep at matched rollout budget, ≥2 models, the table-width-vs-leakage ablation (the "infer all rules from demos" control must not exceed ~50%), per-rule breakdowns, and ceiling certification (agreeing temp-0 repeats) — without these, reviewers will assume the optimizer just transcribed the rule table from a batch.

Realistic venue is a workshop, not a main track

A single-task optimizer-delta result is workshop-scale: an LLM-evaluation / prompt-optimization workshop, or a DSPy/prompt-optimization-adjacent workshop. A Datasets & Benchmarks track (IFBench's home) is plausible only with a full model×optimizer matrix, contamination-proofing story, and released generator — substantially more than a single verified gain. A main conference track is out of reach: the empty cell c11 fills is narrow, and the differentiation from IFBench is an axis-level refinement, not a new paradigm.

Not publishable: leakage-driven gains, noise-band gains, or the degenerate second objective

Recognize early and do not pursue: a gain on the plain-JCS baseline alone with no invented-rule task ever run; a gain that survives only when demo/table-width leakage is permitted; a gain indistinguishable from temp-0 provider nondeterminism; a "two-objective optimization win" claim, since output-bytes is provably degenerate here (correct canonical strings have fixed length) — this validates single-objective optimization plus two-objective plumbing only, not a real multi-objective result; or a single-model, single-optimizer Δ with no rollout-budget normalization.
