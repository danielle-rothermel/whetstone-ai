c19's biggest risk is a naive-prompt floor already too high, not a collapsing task

Five 2024–2026 papers on grid/maze reasoning bound c19's design space.

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-22

Five 2024-2026 papers on grid/maze reasoning bound c19's design space. Only two — LLM-BabyBench's Predict split and the 2026 spatial-world-models study — actually share c19's exact shape (full observation, single call, one derived fact); the other three score full plans or paths, or run an interactive loop, and count as difficulty upper-bounds rather than direct predictors. The convergent warning across all five: c19's biggest risk is a naive-prompt floor that is already too high, not a task that collapses.

What does each paper claim, and does it support the corpus's use of it?

Paper dossiers

GRASP: full-plan foraging where every LLM loses to greedy search

2407.01892
Tang & Kejriwal · arXiv preprint (cs.AI/cs.CL), v1 2024-07, v2 2025-01 · no accepted venue on record

Claim & novelty

16,000 grid instances (11×11, ASCII-rendered) in which a model must emit, in one zero-shot call, a complete up-to-20-action plan to collect energy and return it to start. The paper's headline is that commercial LLMs underperform a greedy-search baseline, and GPT-3.5-Turbo even loses to random walk — evidence that spatial planning, not just spatial description, remains weak. Novelty rests on giving the full grid directly (no NL description step) and scoring autonomous foraging rather than following external instructions.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: full grid plus full constraint text in one prompt, model returns the complete action list with "no further interaction with the environment." The rendered observation is an 11×11 ASCII matrix with coordinate axes 0-10, dashed cell borders, and single characters E/O/A for energy/obstacle/agent.

Models & headline numbers

  • Greedy search beats all 3 LLMs on average energy (+0.35, p<0.001 vs each).
  • GPT-3.5-Turbo underperforms random walk overall (avg energy −2.02 vs random walk's −1.14).
  • GPT-4o is statistically indistinguishable from random walk (t=−0.48, p=0.632).
  • GPT-o1-mini is the only LLM to beat random walk (p<0.001) but still loses to greedy search.
  • The per-step energy-cost constraint causes the single largest score drop for every model tested.

Failure modes

  • GPT-3.5-Turbo: touches energy cells without collecting them, walks through obstacles, never returns to drop energy.
  • GPT-4o: avoids obstacles and moves toward energy but takes unnecessary steps and misses nearby energy.
  • Six documented negative-result categories including looping/oscillation between the same cell pairs and out-of-bounds moves — the same failure family relevant to grid navigation generally.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The "16,000 grid-based environments" figure is exact and verified. But calling GRASP the "direct grid-world antecedent of c19" correction overstates it: GRASP is a multi-step plan-generation and foraging-optimization benchmark, not single-derived-fact state prediction, and has no invented-command or wrap/clamp layer. The corrected framing: GRASP is a relative in the same family, motivational rather than a direct template — that role belongs to LLM-BabyBench's Predict split.

LLM-BabyBench: the Predict split is c19's closest template, and standard semantics don't floor frontier models

2505.12135
Choukrani, Malek, Orel, Xie, Iklassov, Takáč, Lahlou · arXiv preprint, v1 2025-05-17 · no published venue on record

Claim & novelty

A textual BabyAI benchmark suite with three tasks — Predict (final state after an action sequence), Plan (generate actions to reach a subgoal), and Decompose (break a mission into subgoals). Novelty: a purely textual, omniscient interface isolating reasoning from 3D-vision confounds, plus a custom "BabyAI OmniBot" oracle (100% solve rate) as ground truth.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Predict is single-shot, full observation: one prompt with the complete environment (all objects revealed regardless of agent position), initial pose, and an ordered action sequence; the model predicts the final state in one forward pass with "no interaction with the environment... allowed at inference time." Three formats compared — Narrative, Structured bullets, JSON — but none renders a literal ASCII grid; state is always a coordinate-labeled object list.

Models & headline numbers

  • Claude 3.7 Sonnet: Predict success 97.5% (Easy) down to 82.5% (Very Hard) — strongest model, only one holding up on command-sequences (80%).
  • GPT-4o: 97.5% (Easy) → 61.2% (Very Hard).
  • Llama-3.1-70B is a non-monotonic outlier at only 63.8% Easy — below the larger 405B model (85.0%).
  • Llama-3.1-8B: near-floor across the board (12.9% Easy, 2.8% Very Hard).
  • Prompting-format swing: Structured bullets 61.08% > JSON 59.28% > Narrative 56.78% (avg. across models).
  • ToT (62.62%) edges out CoT (61.07%) and zero-shot (59.29%); few-shot gives essentially no lift (59.05%).

Failure modes

  • Maze/multi-room navigation is disproportionately hard vs single-room: DeepSeek-R1-Distill drops from 89% (room) to 44% (maze).
  • Sequences-of-commands break every non-Claude model (Llama-3.1-8B = 0%).
  • Reasoning traces show DeepSeek-R1-Distill "questioning the exactitude" of the grid description rather than just simulating it, unlike Claude.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. Core claim holds: this is exactly the c19 template, and ground truth does trace to expert (OmniBot) trajectories. Precision gap correction: the target_state is a deterministic environment-execution result following the given action sequence, not itself something the OmniBot must "solve" from scratch — the OmniBot machinery centrally serves Plan/Decompose ground truth, and only the input action sequences (not the Predict answer) originate from OmniBot demonstrations.

OpenAlex, fetched 2026-07-22: 0 citations. Mid-2025 preprint, not yet indexed-cited. OpenAlex undercounts relative to Google Scholar; zero here signals "too new to index," not "ignored."

GridRoute: algorithm-in-prompt scaffolding helps path planning, but worked traces are a trade-off, not a win

2505.24306
Li, Tao, Wen, Sun, Gong, Zhang, Xu, Zhang, Ji · arXiv v2 (submitted 2025-05-30, revised 2025-08-13) · not (yet) published at a peer-reviewed venue

Claim & novelty

Benchmarks whether embedding classical pathfinding algorithms (A*, Dijkstra, DFS) directly into the prompt ("Algorithm of Planning," AoP) beats Vanilla/CoT/Few-shot route planning on cardinal-movement grids. Central claim: AoP significantly outperforms Vanilla and rivals CoT, especially as grid size grows. Novel contribution is AoP itself — embedding algorithm mechanics, not just naming the algorithm, distinguishing it from prior PPNL (CoT/ReAct only) and LLM-A* (LLM used only for one sub-step) approaches.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one prompt gives the full obstacle set as rectangle-corner coordinate pairs plus start/end coordinates — the grid is never rendered as an ASCII/visual grid anywhere in the prompt templates. The model returns a complete coordinate-list path in one response; CoT/AoP variants add step-by-step algorithmic instructions or worked examples in that same single prompt.

Models & headline numbers

  • DeepSeek-V3 (671B): one of the most stable performers across all prompt types and sizes (e.g. AoP-A* FR/OR 80/80 at 10×10).
  • ChatGPT-4 (GPT-4 Turbo): Vanilla FR 76 at 10×10, but AoP-DFS collapses it to FR/OR 1-3 at 20×20/30×30.
  • Qwen2.5-7B: Algo-Reasoning (worked-trace) produces CR/FR/OR = 0/0/0 at every map size.
  • Map size (10→20→30) is the dominant driver of decline, independent of model size (Qwen2.5-32B ≈ 72B at 30×30).

Failure modes

Five-way taxonomy: invalid step distance, path through obstacle, out of bounds, empty path, start/end mismatch. As map size grows the dominant error shifts from obstacle-collision to "more intricate" errors; AoP-DFS shows the most severe single collapse recorded (GPT-4 Turbo to FR/OR 1-3 at 20-30×30).

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. Cardinal-movement grid benchmark and AoP algorithm-in-prompt framing are both accurate. But "few-shot worked traces measurably help" correction overstates the finding: Algo-Reasoning is a genuine trade-off — it improves GM/MSE but hurts FR/OR versus the simpler Algo-Direct variant, and produces 0/0/0 outright on the smallest model (Qwen2.5-7B). The corrected claim: worked-trace few-shot trades route-similarity for reduced feasibility/optimality, and can fail entirely on small models.

OpenAlex, fetched 2026-07-22: 1 citation (1 since 2025; 2026:1). Mid-2025 paper. OpenAlex undercounts relative to Google Scholar.

MazeEval: single-author benchmark where every failure is looping, not running out of moves

2507.20395
Hafsteinn Einarsson · arXiv preprint (cs.AI), v1 2025-07-27 · no separate venue/proceedings identified

Claim & novelty

Interactive maze navigation (5×5 to 15×15) using only coordinate feedback and distance-to-wall readings via a function-calling move() interface, no visual input. Across 8 models, O3 navigates perfectly up to 30×30 while every other model fails catastrophically beyond roughly 9×9, and 100% of failures are excessive looping (revisiting a cell 10+ times). A large English-vs-Icelandic performance gap (3-4 size-levels smaller in Icelandic) suggests spatial reasoning is linguistically, not purely mechanistically, grounded.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Interactive, not single-shot: the model calls move(direction) repeatedly; each call returns updated state and the model decides the next move from accumulating history. Per-step observation is local only — distance-to-wall in 4 cardinal directions, current position, goal position, and a full line-by-line travel history — never a global grid rendering. Termination: goal reached, any cell revisited 10+ times, or a 3n² move budget exceeded.

Models & headline numbers

  • O3: perfect success 5×5 through 15×15 (plus an ad hoc 30×30) in both languages; fails at 40×40. Clean ceiling case.
  • "Strong performers" (Claude Opus 4, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro) hold to 8×8/9×9 before degrading.
  • "Weaker performers" (GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4.1-mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash) fail beyond 7×7.
  • Backtracking scales sharply with size: Claude Opus 4 rises from 6.8 avg. backtracks at 5×5 to 68.6 at 12×12.
  • Wall-collision rate stays low even in failed runs (~0.8 avg.) — local per-step reasoning is intact; the failure is integrating history.

Failure modes

Universally: excessive looping from failing to integrate cumulative travel history into a persistent spatial memory, despite the full history being re-supplied every turn — models "treat each navigation decision as relatively independent." Never budget exhaustion. Systematic, not stochastic, across repeated runs.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported, with an author-count correction. The corpus description cited "Einarsson et al." correction — the paper's header lists a single author, Hafsteinn Einarsson, confirmed by direct extraction. The coordinate-based navigation and 9×9 failure-threshold description are accurate as a summary, but the "fail beyond 9×9" framing is a modal/most-common threshold, not universal: it applies mainly to "weaker performers" (fail beyond 7×7-9×9), applies partially to "strong performers" (hold to 8×8/9×9), and does not apply to O3 (clean exception to 30×30).

OpenAlex, fetched 2026-07-22: 0 citations. Mid-2025 preprint, single-author. OpenAlex undercounts relative to Google Scholar.

Do LLMs Build Spatial World Models? Representation format swings accuracy 2-5x, and c19's chosen format is the losing one

2604.10690
Li, Zhu, Das, Dube · arXiv preprint (cs.AI), v1 2026-04-12 · not otherwise published as of fetch date

Claim & novelty

Argues LLMs do not build format-invariant spatial world models: adjacency-list text vastly outperforms visual-grid text for the identical maze, base prompting fails for most models while explicit CoT recovers it, and models treat repeated/compositionally related spatial queries as independent events rather than reusing prior computation — despite 96-99% semantic-coverage similarity in their reasoning traces. Novel probes: Task 2 (exact-repeat query reuse) and Task 3 (compositional distance comparison), designed to test information persistence rather than one-shot accuracy.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot, full observation, across all three tasks. Two formats compared in the same single prompt design: adjacency-list with special tokens (e.g. <ADJLIST_START> (0,0) <--> (0,1)...) versus visual grid (e.g. Row 0: [".", "S", ".", "#"] plus a legend). No image/pixel input tested — both are text.

Models & headline numbers

  • Gemini-2.5-Flash (CoT): 5×5 — 86% adjacency-list vs 34% visual grid; 7×7 — 80% adjacency vs 16% visual. The corpus-cited "16-86%" headline.
  • Claude-Haiku-4.5: near-0% with base prompting at 5×5, recovering to 78% with CoT.
  • GPT-5-mini is the documented exception: no adjacency advantage (30% adj. vs 32% visual at 5×5 CoT); compositional accuracy craters 100%→4% from 5×5 to 9×9.
  • Reasoning traces show 96-99% semantic coverage but ROUGE-L as low as 0.08 (DeepSeek-Chat) — high conceptual similarity without literal reuse.

Failure modes

  • Without CoT, models get "lost in the maze after finding the shortest Euclidean distance," ignoring wall structure — the most common failure mode.
  • Representation brittleness: identical maze, 2-5x accuracy swing from format alone.
  • On an exact-repeat query, Gemini and GPT-5-mini fully re-derive the answer from scratch rather than reusing it.
  • k-shot examples can hurt on larger mazes rather than help.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The 16%/86% swing, the 5×5/7×7 headline figures, and the four model names are all accurate. Two corrections: (1) title correction — the corpus description drops "Evidence from" from the real title ("...Grid-World Maze Tasks" should read "...Evidence from Grid-World Maze Tasks"); (2) grid-size range correction — "5×5 to 7×7" understates scope; the full study spans 5×5 through 9×9, with 5×5-7×7 being only the range highlighted for the specific headline comparison.

OpenAlex, fetched 2026-07-22: 0 citations. 2026 paper — zero-by-construction (indexing lag; a paper this new has had almost no time to accrue or have citations indexed). Not evidence of low impact.

c19's differentiator is output granularity, not the cell

Four of five papers occupy the crowded full-observation × single-shot cell alongside c19; MazeEval alone sits in partial × interactive, and the other two cells are empty. c19 is the only design scoring a single derived fact under strict 0/1 exact match. LLM-BabyBench/Predict is nearest (final position+direction) but grades with Manhattan-distance partial credit and standard BabyAI actions; GRASP and GridRoute score full multi-step plans/paths (many-valid-answer ambiguity); P5's Task 1 scores full-path exact match, conflating planning with state-tracking. The genuinely open territory is combining single-fact exact-match scoring with a non-memorizable invented-command / wrap-vs-clamp convention layer — no paper in this set tests both together.

What accuracy should the c19 baseline expect, and where does it fail?

Capability synthesis

One-shot state prediction is tractable and near-ceiling for strong models at small size; multistep collapses via looping and non-reuse, not budget exhaustion

Single forward-pass state prediction is the easiest cell in this literature: Claude 3.7 Sonnet holds 82.5% even at Very Hard difficulty on LLM-BabyBench's Predict split, and room-level navigation runs 93-98% for strong models. But it is not universal — Llama-3.1-70B is a non-monotonic outlier at 63.8% Easy, and maze/multi-room navigation plus long command sequences degrade every non-Claude model sharply. In multistep/interactive settings the dominant failure is structurally different: 100% of MazeEval's failures are looping (never running out of the move budget), and the 2026 spatial-world-models paper shows models re-derive rather than reuse answers to repeated or compositional queries even within one prompt. Both failure modes are structurally impossible in c19's single-shot single-fact design — there are no repeated turns to lose track of and no cross-query state to fail to reuse. This makes c19 easier than the interactive/batched settings it is compared against, reinforcing rather than easing the too-high-floor risk.

Cheap-tier models are extremely prompt- and format-sensitive: the naive-to-ceiling gap can span 0% to 86%

The cheap tier (Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-5-mini, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-Chat — the exact roster of the 2026 spatial-world-models paper) shows near-total failure under base/no-CoT prompting even at 5×5 (Claude-Haiku and DeepSeek near 0%, Gemini base 8%), but CoT rescues them dramatically (Claude-Haiku 78%, DeepSeek 74%, Gemini 86% at 5×5). GPT-5-mini is a persistent non-conforming outlier, showing no representation advantage and craterring on compositional tasks as size grows. Few-shot is not a reliable lever and can actively hurt (GridRoute's worked-trace prompt produces 0/0/0 on a 7B model; k-shot hurts larger mazes in the 2026 paper) — consensus across three papers is CoT helps, few-shot doesn't.

c19's rendered ASCII grid is the documented weak format

The strongest cross-paper convergence: both papers closest to c19's shape favor structured/symbolic text over rendered/prose grids. The 2026 spatial-world-models paper's central result — adjacency-list beats visual-grid ASCII by 86% vs 34% at 5×5, 80% vs 16% at 7×7 (Gemini-2.5-Flash, CoT) — holds for 3 of 4 models (GPT-5-mini is exempt). LLM-BabyBench separately finds structured bullets (61.08%) beat JSON (59.28%) and narrative prose (56.78%). c19's planned observation — a literal rendered ASCII grid — sits on the losing side of this swing, meaning format choice alone could move c19's ceiling by 30-50 points independent of task difficulty.

What floor and ceiling should the c19 baseline hit?

Floor & ceiling

c19 config: vanilla Minigrid dynamics (no invented commands in the baseline), full-observation ASCII grid, single derived fact, exact-match, temp 0, cheap-tier models. This is the intersection of the easiest task shape in the literature (single fact, ~6×6, no planning/memory-across-turns) and the weakest representation (rendered ASCII) at the most prompt-sensitive tier — no paper tests this exact cell, so both ranges below carry real uncertainty.

Naive floor
~15–55%
Ceiling (ASCII-locked)
~55–75%
Ceiling (structured fmt.)
~80–90%

Uncertainty is high for the floor and medium-high for the ceiling — both anchored to SHOWS data from adjacent tasks (base-prompting/format results on full-path mazes) rather than a direct measurement of single-fact + ASCII + base-prompt + cheap-tier, which no paper tests. Central estimate if the baseline lands mid-range (naive ~30-45%, ceiling ~65-80% on ASCII): usable headroom, no added complexity needed. Go/no-go gate: if measured naive floor exceeds ~60%, add difficulty via c19's own invented-command / wrap-vs-clamp strata, longer command sequences, or 8×8-9×9 sizing before proceeding.

Is the baseline publishable, and against which neighbor?

Positioning & publishability

Highest overlap is LLM-BabyBench/Predict; strip the invented-command layer and c19 is a cheaper re-run

c19's novelty is conditional on its strata mattering: LLM-BabyBench's own data shows five of seven models score 85-97.5% on Easy standard-semantics Predict — direct evidence that standard movement semantics alone do not floor frontier models, which is exactly why c19 needs its invented-command and wrap-vs-clamp strata, and exactly the warning that without them the floor is too high. c19's real differentiators against this paper are narrow but concrete: non-memorizable commands, an explicit wrap/clamp boundary stratum, literal ASCII rendering (LLM-BabyBench never renders one), and strict exact match versus Manhattan-distance partial credit.

Most threatening overlap is the 2026 paper — it already owns the format-sensitivity finding

c19 cannot claim "grids are format-sensitive" as a contribution — the 2026 spatial-world-models paper already delivers that headline empirical result. c19's remaining differentiators are narrow but real: single-fact exact-match isolation of state-tracking (vs. that paper's full-path scoring), and the non-memorizable convention layer it does not test. If c19 wants a contribution distinct from this paper, it must lean on convention-manipulation, not format-sensitivity.

MazeEval is the cleanest complementarity story: c19 isolates the sub-capability MazeEval's own discussion flags as untested

c19 sits in the opposite quadrant from MazeEval on both axes (full x single-shot vs. partial x interactive). MazeEval shows local per-step reasoning stays intact (low wall-collision rate even in failed runs) but cross-turn integration fails via looping; its own Discussion section admits this is untested for the full-observation/single-shot case. c19 directly tests whether that same integration must succeed inside one forward pass, and whether removing the interaction loop (while holding information content equivalent) raises or lowers the failure rate MazeEval attributes to lost spatial memory.

If optimization yields large, verified gains, the precise claim is convention-recovery, not format-sensitivity

The defensible workshop claim: automated prompt optimization (COPRO / MIPROv2 / GEPA) recovers a large fraction of the naive-to-ceiling gap on a contamination-resistant, single-fact task with non-memorizable conventions, with no change to model weights or data, and the gain is attributable to surfacing latent convention rules rather than to memorized grid heuristics or format artifacts. This requires: held-out seed splits, replication across ≥2 optimizers and ≥2 models, and a per-stratum ablation showing gains concentrate on the invented-command/wrap-clamp strata rather than the standard-semantics stratum.

Lead with prompt-optimizer benchmarking, not spatial-reasoning evaluation — and know the early-kill gate

Spatial-reasoning evaluation is a thin line: the 2026 paper already published the format/headroom finding, and the other four papers cover the evaluation angle. The stronger line is prompt-optimizer benchmarking (GEPA is an ICLR 2026 oral; MAS-PromptBench, 2026, is a benchmark specifically for "when does prompt optimization help") — a clean, cheap, auditable task where gains are decomposable per-rule is exactly what that line wants. Not publishable: small or within-variance gains, gains traceable to format artifacts or the standard-semantics stratum rather than convention-learning, or a naive floor already near ceiling (LLM-BabyBench's 85-97% Easy result is a live risk here) — stop before Phase 2 if the naive floor exceeds ~80% on frontier models.

Research-currency verdict: active and current, not yet demonstrably high-impact
