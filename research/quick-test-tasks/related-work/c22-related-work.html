c22's baseline sits in the most crowded corner; its novelty lives in a layer the baseline never exercises

Eleven 2023–2026 constraint-following papers, positioned against c22's stacked-atom micro-generation design.

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-22

Eleven 2023-2026 papers bound c22's design space. c22's un-optimized baseline — standard IFEval checker atoms only, 3-5 stacked per trivial micro-task, strict all-pass 0/1 — sits in the single most crowded corner of this literature (IFEval, COLLIE, FollowBench, InFoBench, ComplexBench, VFF, IFBench all stack deterministic or hybrid constraints and confirm a strict-AND floor effect). c22's genuine novelty — a hidden, discover-not-copy Selection constraint plus a synthetic infinite generator — lives entirely in a later layer the baseline does not yet exercise: every documented Selection operator in this corpus (ComplexBench, IF-RewardBench) states its branch condition in the prompt. The corpus also contains one confirmed citation error, corrected below.

What does each paper claim, and does it support the corpus's use of it?

Paper dossiers

IFEval: the canonical checker library c22 vendors wholesale, at only 1-3 stacked atoms

2311.07911
Zhou, Lu, Mishra, Brahma, Basu, Luan, D. Zhou, Hou (Google) · arXiv preprint, v1 2023-11-14 · no peer-reviewed venue on record

Claim & novelty

Defines 25 verifiable instruction types across 9 groups (keywords, length, casing, format, start/end-with, etc.) whose compliance a simple deterministic program can check, and builds 541 prompts (1-3 instructions each) to score GPT-4 and PaLM 2 Small. Novelty is the taxonomy itself plus a dual strict/loose scoring scheme — loose applies 8 mechanical response transforms (markdown-strip, intro/outro-strip) specifically to fix false negatives from formatting artifacts, at the cost of more false positives.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one prompt with the base request plus 1-3 appended verifiable instructions, one response, no demos shown to the evaluated model. Table 2 example: "Who built the first artificial ice rink? ... Use less than 487 words." Scoring is prompt-level strict-accuracy — all instructions in the prompt must be followed — exactly c22's all-pass 0/1 criterion.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4: prompt-level strict-accuracy 76.89%, inst-level strict 83.57% (Table 3) — the gap between the two numbers is the strict-AND compression effect made visible even at only 1-3 atoms.
  • PaLM 2 Small: prompt-level strict-accuracy 43.07%, inst-level strict 55.76% — a concrete small-model floor at c22's exact scoring convention.
  • Strict vs. loose gap is small but real: GPT-4 +2.41pp, PaLM 2 S +3.88pp prompt-level — a few points of "failure" are formatting false negatives, not true non-compliance.

Failure modes

Naive strict string-matching misclassifies compliant responses as failing when incidental markdown (bold) or intro/outro lines surround an otherwise-correct answer — the exact motivation for the loose metric. Per-category breakdowns exist only in figures not renderable in extraction.

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. IFEval defines exactly 25 verifiable instruction types and is the originating checker-based evaluation approach later work builds on — c22 reuses this checker library directly as its constraint-atom source. One precision note: the paper's own prompt count is 541, not the abstract's rounded "around 500" — immaterial to the corpus claim but worth citing precisely. c22 inherits IFEval's checkers but not its loose-accuracy mitigation, so its strict-only baseline carries the same formatting false-negative risk without the fix.

OpenAlex, fetched 2026-07-22: 30 citations (22 since 2025; 2024:8, 2025:15, 2026:7). Implausibly low for a benchmark of IFEval's stature — read as an indexing artifact, not a true impact signal. OpenAlex undercounts relative to Google Scholar.

COLLIE: one-shot demos don't help, feedback plateaus at 66% — and its grammar has no conditional branch

2307.08689
Yao, Chen, Hanjie, Yang, Narasimhan (Princeton) · arXiv v1 2023-07-17 · ICLR 2024 attribution unconfirmed from arXiv metadata

Claim & novelty

A context-free grammar compositionally expresses count/position constraints across word/sentence/ paragraph/passage levels, combined only with logical AND/OR, with target values extracted from real corpora (Wikipedia, CC-News, Gutenberg) rather than invented. Even GPT-4, the best of five models, satisfies constraints only 50.9% of the time zero-shot on COLLIE-v1.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Primary: zero-shot, one rendered instruction per constraint, one generation, binary True/False per instance. Also runs a genuine interactive feedback loop (rounds 2-4, rule-based or GPT-polished per-turn feedback stating which constraints failed) — the paper's own closest analog to a multistep protocol.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4: 50.9% average zero-shot; 100% success starting a sentence with a given word vs. 40-60% ending one with a given word (position effect).
  • Pass@20: GPT-4 >63% vs. GPT-3.5 32%.
  • Feedback loop: ~20% gain after round 2, then plateaus at 66% through rounds 3-4 — comparable to plain pass@5, not a solve.
  • Logical composition hurts even when the added constraint looks trivial: sent03 (word01 + one extra sentence-level count constraint) is uniformly harder than word01 alone.

Failure modes

Exact-equality count constraints are harder than range or bound-only constraints; arbitrary (non-first, non-last) position constraints are uniformly hard; GPT-4 frequently fabricates non-words ("coordinasor") to satisfy tight letter-position constraints rather than producing valid English.

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The corpus's zero-shot-vs-one-shot numbers (GPT-4 40.7%→39.4%, GPT-3.5 23.1%→23.6%) were run on "an internal version of the dataset before the current Collie-v1" — not COLLIE-v1 itself, and not directly comparable to the 50.9% COLLIE-v1 headline. Also, calling COLLIE "purely synthetic" overstates it: constraint target values are deliberately extracted from real corpora, which the paper explicitly contrasts with prior synthetic grammar-based benchmarks (BabyAI/CLEVR-style).

Partially supported. COLLIE's grammar offers only AND/OR — no conditional Selection operator ("if P then A else B") anywhere in its formal grammar — so it is a structural ancestor of c22's stacking mechanic but not its Selection/discovery mechanism, which is sourced from ComplexBench instead.

FollowBench: the level-ladder c22 borrows, and a judge whose agreement swings 67-88% by prompt design

2310.20410
Jiang, Y. Wang, Zeng, Zhong, Li, Mi, Shang, X. Jiang, Liu, W. Wang (HKUST/Huawei Noah's Ark) · ACL 2024 (arXiv v3 camera-ready 2024-06-05)

Claim & novelty

Five constraint categories (Content, Situation, Style, Format, Example) and a Multi-level mechanism that adds one constraint per level (1-5) to the same base instruction, letting difficulty breakdown localize exactly where a model's instruction-following collapses. A hybrid rule-based/GPT-4-judge protocol shows the instruction-following upper bound for GPT-4/GPT-3.5 is about 3 constraints; open-source models cap around 2.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot, single-round by explicit design ("our current investigation is confined to single-round interactions"): each level-n instance is one independent forward pass with all n constraints stated up front — nothing staged or hidden. The Example-constraint category is itself a 5-shot in-context task with n-1 noise/distractor templates injected as difficulty rises.

Models & headline numbers

  • CSL (Consistent Satisfaction Levels) ceiling: GPT-4 3.3, GPT-3.5 2.9; open-source models cluster 1.4-2.4.
  • Format constraints show the steepest floors: LLaMA2-Chat-70B/13B/7B score 0.0-0.5 HSR across nearly all levels, vs. GPT-4's 87.5→42.5 in the same category.
  • Example constraints (noisy 5-shot) are uniformly hardest: even GPT-4's HSR average is only 50.7, vs. 76-93 in Content/Situation/Style.
  • Judge-prompt ablation: multi-level-aware judge prompt reaches 88% human agreement vs. 79% without the evolution path vs. 67% for a holistic Vicuna-style quality prompt.

Failure modes

All 13 models degrade roughly monotonically 1→5; stronger models fail less consistently once they fail once (GPT-4 42.2% "failure consistency" vs. ChatGLM3-6B's 64.0%) — capability and quality are decomposable axes (WizardLM-13B beats GPT-3.5 on AlpacaEval but trails badly on CSL).

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. The paper verbatim confirms the incremental 1-to-5 level-ladder mechanic c22 borrows. But roughly half of FollowBench's instructions require an LLM judge (an "inherent imperfection" by the authors' own words) — a dependency c22's fully deterministic checker library is designed to avoid entirely. FollowBench's constraints are also all stated in-prompt; its only "discovery" burden falls on the judge (spotting the newly-added constraint), never on the generating model.

InFoBench: DRFR is the ancestor of c22's per-atom diagnostics, but its atoms are judged by an LLM, not code

2401.03601
Qin, Song, Hu, Yao, Cho, X. Wang, Wu, Liu, P. Liu, Yu · arXiv v1 2024-01-07 · Findings of ACL 2024 per task citation (not independently confirmed from arXiv metadata)

Claim & novelty

Introduces the Decomposed Requirements Following Ratio (DRFR): decompose each complex instruction into atomic YES/NO criteria and score per-requirement rather than holistically. A 500-instruction benchmark (Easy + a harder, manually curated Hard Set) pairs with the finding that GPT-4 as annotator is cheap and nearly as accurate as human experts for this decomposed scoring.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Generation is single-shot (one instruction, one greedy-decoded response). Scoring is a multi-turn dialogue with the judge — GPT-4 answers each decomposed question sequentially, carrying prior YES/NO answers as context; this sequential protocol beat both an all-at-once and a fully-independent question format. Few-shot examples added to the judge prompt gave "no significant improvement."

Models & headline numbers

  • DRFR vs. Direct Scoring Pairwise Kappa: 0.532 vs. 0.284 — decomposition roughly doubles annotator agreement.
  • Annotator accuracy/cost (50-instruction slice): Expert 100%/$1,416/1,440 min; GPT-4 89%/$19.40/60 min; AMT 84%/$187.50/2,160 min.
  • Six-model DRFR (Overall): GPT-4 89.4, GPT-3.5 86.7, Claude-2.1 86.4, Gemini-Pro 85.6, Llama-2-70B 84.4, Vicuna-13B 78.4.
  • Even the best model leaves >10% of requirements unfulfilled.

Failure modes

All six models score lowest on Number and Linguistic constraints — miscounted characters, unmaintained numeric ratios; the judge itself inherits some of these numeric-counting errors, plus spatial-reasoning blind spots and susceptibility to instruction-echoing.

Corpus-claim verdict

Supported. DRFR = sum of per-atom binary verdicts over total requirements — precisely a per-atom, partial-credit-style diagnostic model. c22 borrows the per-atom verdict-logging idea but intentionally rejects DRFR's ratio-based partial credit for its own instance score, keeping only strict 0/1 all-pass. The core difference: InFoBench's atoms are judged by an LLM (imperfect per its own error analysis), not a deterministic checker.

ComplexBench: the source of c22's Selection operator — but every documented Selection states its condition

2407.03978
Wen, Ke, Gu, Wu, Huang, Zhou, Li, Hu, Gao, Xu, Liu, Tang, H. Wang, M. Huang (Tsinghua/Zhipu AI/UESTC) · NeurIPS 2024 D&B (arXiv v3, 2024-10-31)

Claim & novelty

Introduces And/Chain/Selection/Nested composition types — beyond simple And-coordination — plus a Rule-Augmented LLM-based (RAL) evaluator combining deterministic rule-checks with an LLM judge. Even GPT-4-1106 fails ~20% of complex instructions overall, and only 14.9% on the strict Coherent Test for multi-layer nested Selection.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Primary: single-shot, one full composed instruction, one response, hybrid RAL scoring (partial-credit DRFR-style, dependency-aggregated — not strict all-pass). Secondary: manually decomposes composition-bearing instructions into a multi-round execution loop.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4-1106: All(Avg.) DRFR 0.800 (best of 15), but Selection(≥3)=0.694 and Sel.&Chain(≥3)=0.626 — its worst subcategories.
  • GPT-3.5-Turbo-1106 fine-grained collapse: DRFR falls from 0.845 (1 And only) to 0.083 for the single hardest combination (1 And + 1 Chain + 2 Selection).
  • Manual decomposition into multi-round execution hurts, not helps: GPT-3.5-Turbo-1106 overall DRFR drops 0.682→0.652, worse on more-composed categories — attributed to cumulative errors across turns.

Failure modes

Selection's difficulty is not just picking the right branch but executing it without interference from irrelevant branches. A documented annotation artifact: ~70% of two-branch Selections have the correct branch first — a positional bias the authors correct for and a direct warning for c22's own Selection generator.

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The operator taxonomy attribution is fully supported, but "discover, not copy" is NOT literally supported by the paper: in every worked example and the formal definition, the Selection condition is always stated explicitly in the instruction text. c22's hidden-condition hardening is a genuine extension this paper does not test or claim.

VFF: structurally the closest paper to the baseline — same strict-AND scoring, same deterministic-beats-judge case

2502.04498
Z. Wang, Jiang, Zhou, Zheng, Zhang, Bansal, Yao (UNC Chapel Hill / Imperial / Microsoft Research) · arXiv v1 2025-02-06 · Findings of NAACL 2025

Claim & novelty

7B-level open models satisfy general instruction-following reasonably but fail badly on fine-grained format control as constraint count rises (level 1→3). VFF curates ~60 Python-verifiable meta-constraints over Alpaca questions and shows a self-improvement pipeline (sample → annotate via checker → SFT+DPO) raises small-model format-following, sometimes past GPT-4.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one instruction (question + concatenated constraints), one response, scored by I = ∏ Fk(y) — strict conjunctive pass across k constraint instances, exactly c22's all-pass design. Multi-step exists only in training (progressive curriculum across difficulty levels), never at evaluation time.

Models & headline numbers

  • GPT-4-turbo strict at levels 1/2/3: 76.29 / 53.33 / 35.31 — the cleanest strict-AND analog to c22's own difficulty dial.
  • GPT-3.5: 62.93 / 34.07 / 16.40; 7B baselines (Mistral, LLaMA-2, LLaMA-3): L1≈50-60, L3≈9-16.
  • Trained LLaMA-3-8B reaches level-3 38.36, edging out GPT-4-turbo's 35.31 — but its general InfoBench score regresses (83.10→78.13).
  • Deterministic checking dominates LLM judging: Python 100% accuracy / 0.52s / $0 vs. GPT-4o-mini 59.0%/99.5s/$0.0144 and GPT-4o 70.0%/205s/$2.38, with judges 25-52% self-inconsistent even at temp 0.1.

Failure modes

All 7B baselines collapse specifically on level-2/level-3 composition, not general instruction comprehension; DPO-only training (no prior SFT) actively harms format-following as training proceeds across levels.

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The paper states "we only reserve about 60 unique meta constraints" — not 64. No occurrence of "64" appears anywhere in the extracted text; the figure should read ~60.

Partially supported, with the numeric correction above. The low small-model floor on composed constraints is directly confirmed. VFF has no conditional/Selection constraint anywhere — its meta-constraints are static template-fills, the single largest structural gap versus c22's design, and its base task is open-ended Alpaca prose, not c22's trivial micro-output.

LIFEBench: exact length targets are the hardest control method — and c22's tiny output sidesteps this entirely

2505.16234
Wei Zhang, Zhou, K. Wang, Fang, Y. Zhang, R. Wang, G. Zhang, Li, Sun, Lyu, Y. Liu, Su (BUPT/NTU/NUS/M-A-P/NCEPU/Sony AI) · NeurIPS 2025 (arXiv v1 2025-05-22)

Claim & novelty

The first full-range (16-8,192 words) length-instruction-following benchmark: 26 LLMs mostly fail to hit exact length targets, and almost universally fail to reach vendor-claimed maximum output lengths even under extreme forcing (up to 32,768 words). Introduces Length Deviation/Length Score metrics and a "lazy generation strategies" failure taxonomy.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: one templated [Instruction] + [Requirement] prompt, one generation, scored post hoc by word count. No demos, no feedback loop in the main protocol. Reasoning models show internal multi-step self-correction (draft, self-count, revise) within one call, not an external interaction loop.

Models & headline numbers

  • 23 of 26 models score below Length Score 60 under Equal-To (exact match); only o3-mini (75.4), Claude-3.7-Sonnet-Thinking (61.3), Gemini-2.5-Pro (60.0) exceed it.
  • Under At-Most, 19/26 exceed LS 90; under At-Least, only 6/26 do — "Equal To" is far harder than one-sided bounds.
  • Under long constraints (≥4096 words) no model consistently exceeds LS 40.
  • Lazy-strategy rate (refusal / partial-attempt) exceeds 10% on average at the 8192-word constraint, near 50% for o1-mini/o3-mini specifically.

Failure modes

Length-awareness deficit: models cannot accurately gauge their own generated word count; reasoning-model self-correction "only partially alleviates the problem for short length constraints and still fails under longer constraints."

Corpus-claim verdict

correction, confirmed First author is Wei Zhang (BUPT) — the paper's own header cross-check explicitly confirms this and flags an earlier "Wu" citation in the corpus as an error.

Partially supported. The low floor is real but is not exclusively a small-model effect — even large/proprietary/reasoning models (GPT-4o, Gemini-2.0-Flash, DeepSeek-R1) score well below 60 LS under exact-match. Relevant to c22: its micro-output design ("a few words") makes an exact-word-count atom trivially satisfiable on a tiny target, sidestepping LIFEBench's central long-form collapse.

IFBench: frontier models score <50% OOD after saturating IFEval at 80%+ — c22's baseline reuses the saturated atoms

2507.02833
Pyatkin, Malik, Graf, Ivison, Huang, Dasigi, Lambert, Hajishirzi (AI2 / University of Washington) · NeurIPS 2025 D&B (arXiv v3, 2025-11-11)

Claim & novelty

Frontier models (GPT-4.1, Claude 3.7/4 Sonnet, Qwen3-32B) strongly overfit to IFEval's saturated 25-constraint set and score below 50% on 58 new OOD constraints despite 80%+ on IFEval. RLVR training (IF-RLVR/GRPO) on stacked constraints — up to 5-6 per training instance — improves both in-domain and OOD instruction-following.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-turn: task + 1-2 concatenated constraints, one completion. Multi-turn variant: constraint deferred to a third dialogue turn asking the model to rewrite a prior response — still one generation call, with dialogue history as context rather than an interactive feedback loop. The shipped eval set contains only 1-2 constraints per instance — deeper stacking (up to 6) appears only in training data, not the released benchmark.

Models & headline numbers

  • Frontier models: <50% IFBench vs. 80%+ IFEval — the paper's headline generalization gap.
  • Training-stacking sweep: IFBench score peaks at n=3 stacked training constraints (59.5), non-monotonic thereafter (49.4 at n=4, 55.8 at n=5) — direct empirical support for c22's 3-5 dial.
  • Multi-turn transfer: models trained only on single-turn data lose ~20-30 points when evaluated multi-turn; only mixed-format training balances both.
  • Category floors persist post-training: "words"/"sentence" categories stay 48-71% even after targeted RL, vs. casing/format 90+.

Failure modes

Reward hacking: IF-RLVR-trained models over-prioritize the verifiable constraint at the expense of task quality (e.g., "only use unique words" wrecks an actual tiramisu recipe); base-policy completions score higher on LLM-judged quality (7/10) than IF-RLVR completions (6.4/10) on the same prompts.

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The extracted paper text contains no mention of "GEPA" anywhere — the "GEPA's headline IF task" clause cannot be verified from this source and is presumably attested elsewhere.

Partially supported on the 58-OOD-constraints-plus-verifiers half. c22's un-optimized baseline uses exactly the saturated IFEval atoms IFBench shows are overfit — the family is current, but the baseline's specific atom set is its least-current slice.

IF-RewardBench: judges max out at 0.609 tau_b vs. 0.755 human — and corroborates Selection as the hardest composition to verify

2603.04738
B. Wen, Niu, C. Wang, Ling, Y. Zhang, Ke, H. Wang, M. Huang (Tsinghua CoAI / Zhipu AI / UESTC) · ACL 2026 (arXiv v2, 2026-04-16)

Claim & novelty

Benchmarks judge/reward models, not generators: even the best proprietary judge (Gemini-3-Pro) reaches only Kendall tau_b 0.609 ranking responses by instruction-following quality, versus a human baseline of 0.755; all dedicated reward models score below 0.2. Adopts ComplexBench's And/Chain/Selection/Nested taxonomy directly.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single inference pass per judgment: full instruction + full constraint checklist + response, judge emits all per-constraint verdicts at once (constraint assessment) or ranks multiple responses (overall assessment). Not a generator benchmark — this paper studies whether a judge can score outputs correctly, a different axis from c22 entirely.

Models & headline numbers

  • 74.6% of (response, constraint) pairs are annotated "followed" — a rare direct anchor for a single stated constraint's base pass rate, used as c22's floor-risk calibration anchor.
  • Complex composition types (Chain, Selection) are harder for judges to verify than Single/And — independent corroboration that Selection resists easy reasoning, this time from the judge-reliability angle.
  • Disabling chain-of-thought hurts judges substantially (GLM-4.6 constraint-assessment tau_b drops 32.7%); self-consistency (Maj@K) helps but saturates beyond K=7.
  • Numerical/Format constraints — c22's exact atom set — are the easiest, most reliably verified category; Situation/Style are hardest and least suited to automatic judgment.

Failure modes

Judges systematically under-detect constraint violations (low negative-class F1) and fail to correctly prioritize system-prompt constraints over conflicting later user-turn constraints.

Corpus-claim verdict

Partially supported. The 842-instruction count and three instruction types are directly confirmed. But "extending the verifiable-constraint atom pool" overstates it: most of IF-RewardBench's constraint categories are not reliably code-verifiable and rely on costly human annotation (~$15,000, 22 annotators) — it extends the constraint taxonomy and judge-evaluation methodology, not a deterministic-checker code pool comparable to IFEval/IFBench's.

The Instruction Hierarchy: a foundational rule-priority framework, but cited into this corpus via a shaky reference

2404.13208
Wallace, Xiao, Leike, Weng, Heidecke, Beutel (OpenAI) · arXiv v1 2024-04-19 · no peer-reviewed venue on record

Claim & novelty

LLMs are vulnerable to prompt injection largely because they treat system, user, and tool-output instructions as equal priority. Proposes a 4-level hierarchy (System > User > multimodal > Tool) and a synthetic-data fine-tuning recipe, improving robustness by up to 63% in-domain and 34% on held-out attack generalization, with minimal capability loss.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single forward-pass: System + User (+ optional Tool Output) in, one Model Output, scored by exact-string/ unigram-overlap heuristics or a separate GPT-4-judge call — never a deterministic per-atom checker.

Models & headline numbers

  • Hierarchy-trained GPT-3.5 Turbo: up to 63% robustness gain on main evals, 34% on held-out generalization evals, comparable capability benchmarks (TriviaQA, LAMBADA, HellaSwag).
  • Regressions on two over-refusal evals (System Message Probing Questions; Jailbreakchat with Allowed Prompts) — the hierarchy over-applies to benign look-alike inputs.
  • A prompting-only "System Message Baseline" is much weaker than fine-tuning on hierarchy data, though combining both sometimes helps most.

Failure modes

Over-refusal is the central failure mode; the Conclusion also concedes "our current models are likely still vulnerable to powerful adversarial attacks."

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The extraction contains no reference to "IHEval" anywhere in the body or references list — this paper does not mention, propose, or evaluate on an IHEval benchmark. c22's own candidate page separately flags that the corpus cites it only via an OpenReview id, not the arXiv page — an independent citation-hygiene issue corroborating that the IHEval linkage needs a downstream-paper check, not this one.

Partially supported. The foundational privileged-instruction framework is real and well-supported; the paper's scoring (GPT-4-judge / string-overlap) is exactly the kind of non-deterministic verification c22's checker-library design is built to avoid.

DeonticBench: rule-selection dominates real-world failure (96.8% "Wrong Rule" on Housing) — adjacent, not a direct ancestor

2604.04443
Dou, Brena, Deo, Jurayj, J. Zhang, Holzenberger, Van Durme (Johns Hopkins / Télécom Paris) · arXiv v1 2026-04-06 · venue ambiguous (COLM-styled, unconfirmed)

Claim & novelty

LLMs struggle to apply obligations/permissions/prohibitions from long real statutes across four high-stakes domains (tax, airline fees, immigration appeals, housing law), even with an optional Prolog solver-assisted workflow. 6,232 tasks, the largest benchmark to date for executable rule-based reasoning in real-world domains.

Protocol & observation (exact)

Single-shot: Direct / Zero-Shot / Few-Shot, one call each, scored by an external SWI-Prolog execution engine comparing printed output to a gold label ($1 tolerance for numeric, exact match for classification) — a real symbolic verifier, but one that checks only the final printed answer, with no per-sub-rule diagnostic built into scoring itself.

Models & headline numbers

  • Best hard-subset results: O3 Zero-Shot 44.4% on SARA Numeric; GPT-4.1 Few-Shot 46.6 macro-F1 on Housing — though within the same table GPT-5.1 Few-Shot scores higher on Housing (46.8), and a separately-tabulated coding model (GPT-5.2-Codex) scores higher on SARA Numeric (45.8).
  • Error taxonomy on hardest failures: "Wrong Rule" dominates Housing (96.8% few-shot) and USCIS-AAO (77.3% zero-shot); "Numerical" (arithmetic) errors dominate Airline (100% few-shot).
  • SFT/DPO/GRPO on Qwen2.5-32B improves classification tasks but SARA Numeric stays below 10% across every training method.

Failure modes

Symbolic (Prolog) generation trades abstentions for correctness vs. Direct prompting, which reduces abstentions but raises incorrect-answer rates; increased reasoning effort does not consistently help and sometimes hurts.

Corpus-claim verdict

correction The "successor to the RuleArena rule-application family" framing overstates the relationship: RuleArena is only one of four source domains (the Airline subset) — the paper itself frames RuleArena/CL-bench merely as "closest to our setting" for a size comparison, not as lineage DeonticBench formally succeeds. The paper never mentions IFEval/IFBench/ verifiable-constraint instruction-following at all; "adjacent to c22" is the corpus's own editorial framing.

Partially supported. The 6,232-task count and optional-Prolog-verifier framing are confirmed. Its "Wrong Rule" error category echoes c22's Selection mechanism at a conceptual level (discover which rule applies) but at a heavyweight symbolic-execution scale, contrasting with c22's lightweight pure-Python checkers.

Where does c22 sit in the family, and which cells are empty?

Positioning map

Three axes organize this family: verifier type (deterministic code → hybrid → LLM judge → symbolic execution), rule provenance (stated atoms → stated composition → hidden/discover → source-priority), and output shape (micro all-pass vs. free-form partial-credit). Every one of the 11 papers is single-shot on the generation side — "multi-turn" is always a judge protocol, a training curriculum, or a difficulty ladder, never an interactive solver loop for the model under test.

Crowded region

Deterministic/hybrid scoring × stated stacked atoms × free-form output. IFEval, COLLIE, VFF, IFBench, FollowBench, and ComplexBench's rule subset all live here, all use constraint count as the difficulty dial, and all confirm the strict-AND floor effect plus deterministic-beats-judge. c22's un-optimized baseline lands squarely in this corner.

Empty cell 1 — trivial micro-output

No paper deliberately trivializes the base task to isolate constraint-following in strict all-pass scoring over a few-word output. Every other paper uses substantive base tasks (essays, recipes, statutes) where task-quality and constraint-following interact.

Empty cell 2 — hidden Selection

The Selection operator exists (ComplexBench, IF-RewardBench), but in every documented example the branch condition is stated in the prompt. c22's "discover, not copy" conditional constraint over trivial micro-outputs occupies a genuinely empty cell.

Empty cell 3 — seeded infinite generator

Every paper here ships a fixed, frozen, released dataset. None ships a seeded on-the-fly generator. c22's infinite/contamination-resistant pool is a real gap — but it is build burden, not the novelty claim: the checker code is reused; the generator is net-new, unshipped work.

What accuracy should the reseed-only baseline expect?

Capability synthesis

Scoped to c22's reseed-only baseline: standard IFEval checker atoms only, no OOD/invented atoms, no Selection constraints yet — the framing that removes exactly the two mechanisms carrying c22's anti-enumeration story, so the evidence that applies most directly is the IFEval/IFBench-in-domain/VFF/FollowBench body of work.

One-shot works at 1-2 atoms; strict all-pass collapses by 4-5 — at every model tier

Hard anchors at c22's exact scoring convention: IFEval prompt-strict at 1-3 atoms — GPT-4 76.9%, PaLM-2-Small 43.1%. VFF strict at 3 atoms — GPT-4-turbo 35%, GPT-3.5 16%, 7B models 9-16%. Per single stated constraint, base pass rate is ≈0.75 (IF-RewardBench) — casing/format/keyword atoms are easy (90+ even for mid models), exact-count/forbidden-letter/length atoms are the hard floor-drivers. c22's trivial micro-output uniquely neutralizes the length/counting-collapse that dominates VFF/LIFEBench (a 2-word answer trivially satisfies "≤N words"), easing it relative to those benchmarks on the count/length axis specifically.

Multistep does not rescue: few-shot ≈ 0 effect, decomposition hurts, feedback plateaus

Few-shot/one-shot demonstrations do not help and can hurt: COLLIE zero→one-shot is statistically indistinguishable; FollowBench's Example category (5-shot by design) is its hardest category. Instruction decomposition into multi-turn execution actively hurts (ComplexBench: GPT-3.5 DRFR 0.682→0.652, attributed to cumulative multi-round errors). Iterative feedback helps early then plateaus without solving (COLLIE: +20% at round 2, then flat at 66% through round 4). This supports c22's single-call design but warns the task will not be "fixed" by prompting scaffolds — difficulty is intrinsic to strict compositional constraint-following.

Cheap-tier models are the decisive band, and the evidence here is thinnest: estimates interpolate between GPT-3.5/PaLM-2-Small and GPT-4

No dossier reports numbers for c22's exact cheap tier (Gemini-2.5-Flash / GPT-5-mini / Claude Haiku 4.5 / DeepSeek-Chat class) on a strict all-pass, deterministic-checker micro-task — the single largest evidence gap in this synthesis. The closest anchors: PaLM-2-Small IFEval prompt-strict 43.07% at 1-3 atoms; GPT-3.5 VFF strict 62.93/34.07/16.40 at L1/L2/L3; 7B-8B open models consistently L1≈50-65, L3≈9-16. Today's cheap-tier hosted models are almost certainly stronger than these proxies but weaker than frontier — placed between GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4: closer to GPT-4 on easy atoms (casing/format/keyword), closer to GPT-3.5 on hard atoms (exact-count/forbidden-letter) and under deep stacking. The cheap tier should show the steepest count-driven decay of the deployable tiers, mirroring VFF's GPT-3.5 curve (63→34→16) more than its GPT-4 curve (76→53→35).

What floor and ceiling should the reseed-only baseline hit?

Floor & ceiling

Setup: cheap tier, temp 0, strict all-pass, 3-5 stated IFEval atoms sampled fresh per instance over a trivial micro-task. This is the intersection of the easiest task shape in the literature (micro-output neutralizes the length/count confound) and the least-measured model tier — no dossier tests this exact cell, so both ranges carry real, asymmetric uncertainty.

Naive-prompt floor
~0.20–0.45
Ceiling (engineered prompt)
~0.55–0.80

Point estimates: naive floor ≈0.30 (between VFF-L3 GPT-3.5's 0.16 and IFEval's 1-3-atom small-model 0.43, nudged up by the micro-task easing, down by 4-5-atom strictness and c22's strict-only formatting false-negatives); ceiling ≈0.70 (an engineered prompt enumerating each stated atom should approach a strong model's per-atom competence, since nothing is hidden in the reseed-only baseline). Uncertainty is HIGH and asymmetric — the floor could be as low as ~0.10 (hard-atom-heavy sampler, fast cheap-tier decay) or as high as ~0.50 (easy-atom-skewed sampler); the ceiling is more stable (0.55-0.85). Go/no-go gate: if the measured naive floor exceeds ~0.60, add difficulty via count/hard-atom strata before proceeding.

The gap is real but likely one-shot-closable: the reseed-only baseline strips its own anti-enumeration mechanisms

A naive-to-ceiling gap of ~0.30-0.45 (0.30→0.70) is plausible and would give a usable optimization signal. But the reseed-only baseline is explicitly missing the Selection/discovery and OOD atoms — the two mechanisms c22's own verdict says carry the entire anti-enumeration load. Because every constraint is stated in this baseline, a single well-drafted enumerate-the-atoms prompt can in principle close most of that gap: nothing needs discovering. This predicts the reseed-only baseline will look promising on floor-vs-ceiling spread but weak on sustained optimization difficulty — evidence leans toward needing the Selection/OOD complexity layer for a durable optimizer target, unless the count/hard-atom strata alone are found to hold up a residual gap.

Is the baseline publishable, and as what?

Positioning & publishability

The un-optimized baseline adds almost nothing over IFEval/VFF/IFBench as an IF benchmark

Every per-paper comparison in this corpus reaches the same conclusion for the baseline specifically: c22 reuses IFEval's checkers verbatim, VFF already tests strict-AND stacking over parametrized meta-constraints, and IFBench already shows those same atoms are overfit/saturated. c22's honest additions at the baseline stage are narrow — a seeded generator vs. frozen datasets, deliberate base-task triviality, and per-instance per-checker diagnostic logging — not new instruction-following science. The novelty (Selection/discovery, OOD atoms, infinite generator) is entirely a later layer the baseline does not yet exercise.

If optimization yields large, verified gains, the precise claim is a measurement claim, not a capability claim

The defensible framing: reflective prompt optimization (GEPA/MIPROv2/COPRO) raises strict all-pass accuracy from X% to Y% at Z rollouts on a deterministically-verified, contamination-resistant task, with zero label noise because scoring is execution-based (VFF: Python 100% vs. judges 59-70% with 25-52% self-inconsistency). This is not a claim that optimizers improve instruction-following capability in general — only that they improve this measured metric on this task.

Belongs to the prompt-optimizer-benchmarking line, realistically a workshop paper — not a new IF-benchmark venue

Framed as "a new IF benchmark," the baseline would be correctly rejected as incremental over the crowded A1×B1×C2 corner (per-paper analysis above shows it adds nothing over IFEval/VFF/IFBench there). Its real value is as a clean testbed for comparing optimizers: deterministic oracle, tunable difficulty, contamination-proof, cheap — exactly the current prompt-optimizer-benchmarking line (GEPA is an ICLR 2026 oral whose headline IF task is IFBench, c22's lineage). Reviewers will demand: multiple optimizers at matched rollout budgets, a few-shot control (since COLLIE/InFoBench/IFEval all show demos are cheap here), difficulty-stratified and per-atom curves, held-out seed/atom generalization, multiple base models, and variance reporting — and will reject floor-artifact, memorized-atom, or judge-scored results. A NeurIPS D&B path (the IFBench/ComplexBench precedent) needs the full Selection+OOD+generator design, not the baseline.

Research-currency verdict: the family is squarely current, but the baseline's atom set is its most saturated layer
