###### Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents in scientific tasks. Yet whether these systems can effectively engage in forms of inductive reasoning relevant to scientific discovery remains an open question. In this work, we introduce FalsifyBench, an evaluation framework for hypothesis-driven reasoning inspired by the classic Wason 2-4-6 task, in which agents must discover hidden semantic properties by iteratively proposing examples and receiving feedback.
This task captures key elements of scientific reasoning: hypothesis generation, evidence gathering, and belief revision in response to both confirming and disconfirming evidence. Our evaluation of 12 LLMs across model families and scales shows that reasoning models are generally stronger scientific reasoners than instruction-tuned models, although no model comes close to optimal performance. The primary driver of success is the capacity for negative testing: models that actively seek to falsify their hypotheses consistently outperform those that primarily seek confirmation. Moreover, a fine-grained turn-level analysis, neglected in previous work, reveals that failure is tied to identifiable patterns in how models navigate the hypothesis space.
##
1 Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) are becoming increasingly capable across domains associated with scientific work, including literature review (Mialon et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2025), software engineering and programming (Jimenez et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2025), mathematics (Mirzadeh et al., 2025; Glazer et al., 2025), and expert-level knowledge (Wang et al., 2024; Rein et al., 2024; Center for AI Safety et al., 2026). These advances have motivated a growing body of work on autonomous agents capable of performing scientific research in an end-to-end manner (Lu et al., 2024; Gottweis et al., 2025). As these systems become increasingly integrated into scientific workflows, a fundamental question arises: can they carry out the key forms of inductive reasoning required for scientific research? Central among these is the ability to generate, evaluate, and revise hypotheses in light of new evidence. A critical component of this process is falsification, whereby a hypothesis is rejected in favor of an alternative when it is contradicted by the evidence.

One of the best-known and most widely used paradigms for studying this process in humans is the Wason 2-4-6 task (Wason, 1960). Participants are shown a triple of numbers (e.g., “2, 4, 6”) and asked to discover the hidden rule underlying it, denoted here as RR. They do so by proposing new triples and receiving feedback from the experimenter on whether each triple fits the rule. Once sufficiently confident that they have discovered RR, participants state their hypothesis (denoted here as HH). When the rule to be discovered is more general than the one suggested by the initial example (as in Wason’s original task, where RR is “Any increasing sequence of three numbers”), success rates are low. This is because participants tend to form overly specific hypotheses (e.g., “Even numbers increasing by two”). In such cases, generating triples consistent with specific hypotheses (e.g., “8, 10, 12”) cannot reveal that those hypotheses are too narrow and falsify them. This behavior has been termed Confirmation bias and has traditionally been interpreted as an irrational tendency that runs counter to scientific reasoning.

Later work by Klayman and Ha (1987) has made a more nuanced distinction between positive testing, in which one seeks evidence expected to confirm the current hypothesis, and negative testing, in which one seeks evidence expected to disconfirm it. Crucially, they showed that positive testing can also lead to the falsification of a hypothesis and that the effectiveness of these two strategies depends on the relation between HH and RR.
Specifically, they showed that a positive test strategy can falsify a hypothesis HH when it is broader than the hidden rule RR (H⊃R), while it fails to falsify HH whenever this is a strict subset of RR (H⊂R). In the latter case, negative testing is the only strategy that can conclusively falsify HH. Table 1 provides a summary of all possible relations between HH and RR, together with the type of test that can conclusively falsify HH in each case.111We include a detailed discussion of cases where HH can be conclusively falsified, with visual examples, in Appendix A.

In this work, we introduce a generalized version of the Wason task in which LLMs must discover hidden semantic properties rather than numerical rules. In this semantic version, we exploit taxonomic relations to build games emphasizing the H⊂R configuration, where negative testing is the optimal strategy and which corresponds to Wason’s original case.
This configuration is particularly interesting because it mirrors a recurring pattern in the history of science, where accepted theories later turn out to be special cases of broader ones (e.g., Newtonian mechanics as a limiting case of relativity). This parallel motivates our focus on the falsification and revision of overly narrow hypotheses through negative testing as a key component of scientific reasoning, and one worth testing in systems such as LLMs. At the same time, following Klayman and Ha (1987), we do not assume that negative testing is universally superior. Instead, we examine how LLMs handle hypothesis testing across different relations between HH and RR and apply the most appropriate normative analysis to each case, as described below.

Our main contributions are as follows: (1) we introduce FalsifyBench222The code and data used for this paper are publicly available at https://github.com/leobertolazzi/FalsifyBench.git., a benchmark for hypothesis-driven reasoning grounded in semantic taxonomies, which operationalizes a key component of scientific reasoning: the ability to actively generate, and potentially falsify, a current hypothesis in order to control for it; (2) we evaluate 12 LLMs across multiple model families and scales, showing that reasoning models generally behave as stronger scientific thinkers than instruction-tuned models in our framework, although no model approaches optimal performance; and (3) we run a fine-grained turn-level analysis, made possible by our evaluation setting, showing that failures reflect identifiable patterns in how models navigate the hypothesis space.

##
2 Related Work

Inductive reasoning
is central to a wide range of human activities, including empirical scientific research, and has therefore become a key focus in evaluations of reasoning in LLMs.

Early studies have tested this ability in sequence-to-sequence models using synthetic benchmarks.
Chollet (2019) introduced the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC), which tests models on grid transformation tasks, in which they must infer abstract transformations from a few example input-output pairs and learn to apply the correct rule to unseen grids. Lake and Baroni (2018) take a similar approach, testing the capability to learn mappings from commands to action strings, and testing their application to novel, unseen compositions of sequences.

Recent work has increasingly focused on understanding how LLMs perform inductive reasoning beyond task performance. Jin et al. (2026) showed that chain-of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022) does not universally improve induction and can even reduce performance. Li et al. (2025) introduced a framework for evaluating and explaining inductive reasoning in LLMs by disentangling induction and deduction steps. Louapre (2026) examined how LLMs behave in an inductive game, explicitly mapping models’ strategies to human-like traits that influence performance, and connecting their setting to scientific discovery.

Within this line of research, our work is closely related to studies that also draw inspiration from Wason’s 2-4-6 task. Banatt et al. (2024) introduce WILT, a multi-turn inductive logic benchmark where hidden rules are boolean functions involving three variables; Jhaveri et al. (2026) use rules about number properties and explicitly study Confirmation bias in LLMs, applying interventions studied in humans to LLMs to encourage falsificatory behavior. Neither study explicitly takes into account the normative framework introduced by Klayman and Ha (1987):
they neither sample triples from a rule that is a strict subset of the general target rule nor track how the hypotheses formed by models at each turn relate to the target rule. In our experiment, we instead address this limitation by sampling triples of terms from nodes that are strict taxonomic descendants of RR, thereby making negative testing the better strategy for identifying it.

##
3 Method

###
3.1 Experiment Overview

In our experiment, each LLM served in two roles: as the oracle, which answered queries, and as the player, which attempted to discover the hidden rule. In direct analogy with Wason’s original experiment, the oracle provided the player with an initial triple of terms sampled from a subordinate semantic category (e.g., “vertebrate”), which was itself included in a more general target category (e.g., “animal”). The more general target category served as the target rule RR. By iteratively proposing new examples and receiving feedback, the player accumulated evidence, testing its current hypothesis either positively or negatively, until it could identify RR. An example of a real game is shown in Figure 1. More specifically, on each turn, the player proposed three items along with a hypothesis and a rationale for why those items were chosen. The oracle responded with the string “Conform” or “Do not conform” depending on whether the set of items satisfied the target rule RR. When sufficiently confident, the player submitted a final answer, and the oracle responded with either “Correct” or “Incorrect”. Thus, the player was prompted to use Test actions to gather information and refine its hypotheses, and to use Guess actions once enough information had been collected. When the player submitted an incorrect guess, it learned that its current hypothesis was wrong and was prompted to perform new actions to gather additional evidence until it was confident enough to make a new guess. A game ended when either the player submitted a correct guess or the maximum turn limit was reached (we set the maximum number of turns to 20).

We chose semantic categories, rather than numerical sequences, as the domain of our experiment for two reasons: LLMs are very likely to have encountered the classic 2-4-6 task during pretraining, and semantic taxonomies allow us to define a rich set of candidate concepts with increasingly specific subconcepts. The categories were extracted from WordNet (Miller, 1995), which enables the construction of a large number of such games by selecting general concepts and sampling from their descendants. From this large space, we curated a set of 100 representative games spanning different taxonomic depths and semantic domains.

Each game was defined by two semantic categories: the first was the general category that served as the target hidden rule RR, while the second was a sampling category, SS, a descendant of RR from which the initial examples were drawn. For instance, if R=R= “animal” and S=S= “vertebrate”, a possible set of items was E=E= {“pocketed bat”, “skimmer”, “tarsius glis”}. The player observed these items and formed an initial hypothesis, H1H_{1}. Because the items were drawn from the node SS, which was deep in the taxonomy, semantic categories close to that region tended to appear more plausible than very general ones, like RR, so players typically began with a hypothesis that was more specific than the true rule.333This pattern mirrors Wason’s findings, where participants exposed to the sequence “2-4-6” favored overly specific rules such as “increasing even numbers” instead of the true rule “any increasing sequence”.
When HH was more specific than RR, positive testing always returned positive feedback. Making progress therefore required negative testing: only by proposing items outside the current hypothesis could the player determine that RR was broader than hypothesized and revise the hypothesis upward toward it.

Overall, we selected 7 high-level WordNet categories as candidate target rules: “animal”, “artifact”, “body part”, “food”, “location”, “plant”, and “worker”. For each, we identified all descendant subcategories and sampled triples from their leaves, yielding 17,905 total games. Since many WordNet subcategories involve low-frequency vocabulary, we manually filtered the subcategory pool and created a curated set of 100 games spanning 5 categories: “animal”, “artifact”, “body part”, “food”, and “plant”. Full details on game construction and the complete list of selected sampling categories appear in Appendix B. The results we report in this paper are collected on this curated set of 100 games.

The two roles assumed by each LLM (i.e, player and oracle) were instantiated through separate API calls. This allowed us to evaluate models not only on hypothesis-driven reasoning, but also on their ability to provide semantic judgments consistent with the taxonomic knowledge encoded in WordNet. We forced both agents to produce actions in JSON format to constrain the space of their possible outputs. We provide more details on the exact prompts and JSON schema used in our experiments in Appendix C.

###
3.2 Metrics

We evaluate performance of LLMs on FalsifyBench using the following metrics.

#### Player Success Rate.

We computed the percentage of games in which the player correctly identified the target rule RR. This served as the primary measure of task performance, indicating whether the model could converge on the correct hypothesis.

#### Player Confirmation Bias.

We tracked the proportion of positive tests among all tests performed within a game to assess whether models instantiated a general positive testing strategy, as humans often do in the original Wason task. We then averaged this proportion across games and refer to it as Confirmation bias. By construction, in our experiment, positive tests were expected to be largely ineffective at falsifying the current hypothesis, making this metric a direct measure of the extent to which a model pursues an uninformative strategy.

#### Oracle Taxonomic Knowledge.

Since each LLM served in both roles (i.e., as player and as oracle), we quantified the oracle’s taxonomic knowledge to assess whether potentially inaccurate feedback could contribute to poor player performance. To this end, for each game and each model, we randomly sampled one triple from the Test turns and one hypothesis from the Guess turns.444With 100 games and 12 models, this yields 1200 Test instances and 1200 Guess instances. A human annotator then labeled the oracle’s feedback for each triple and each guess according to whether it conformed to RR. The annotator was given access to the NLTK library (Bird and Loper, 2004) and to search engines to ensure that annotations accurately reflected the taxonomic relations encoded in WordNet. We evaluated oracle performance by measuring its accuracy and agreement with the human annotations, using Cohen’s κ\kappa.

##
4 Performance on FalsifyBench

Our evaluation across 12 models revealed substantial variation in player and oracle performance, which we analyze below.

#### Player Performance and Confirmation Bias.

Reasoning models generally achieved higher success rates than their instruction-tuned counterparts. The best instruction-tuned model, DeepSeek-V3.1, reached only 41% success rate, while top reasoning models achieved 75% (GPT-5.2-Chat) and 68% (GPT-OSS-120B), with the remaining instruction-tuned models clustering between 33% and 35%. Notably, success rates remained far from saturated even for the best model, leaving substantial room for improvement. The advantage of reasoning models did not uniformly extend to the smallest ones: GPT-5-Nano (12%) and Qwen3.5-9B (21%) performed worse than all instruction-tuned models, suggesting that extended thinking alone was insufficient at smaller scales.
Additionally, we found a strong correlation between success rates and Confirmation bias: models that predominantly used positive testing consistently underperformed those that actively tested outside their current hypothesis (Spearman ρ=−0.779\rho=-0.779, p=0.003p=0.003). This pattern held across both model families and scales, with stronger reasoning models maintaining substantially lower Confirmation bias ratios (GPT-5-Mini: 39.0%, GPT-5.2-Chat: 27.3%, GPT-OSS-20B: 24.2%) compared to instruction-tuned models, which clustered between 66% and 82%.

#### Oracle Quality.

In terms of oracle performance with respect to human annotation, raw accuracy was moderately high across all models, with most LLMs clustering between 85% and 95%. Cohen’s κ\kappa revealed more meaningful variation, spanning from 0.34 (Mistral-Small-24B, Guess turns) to 0.98 (GPT-5.2-Chat, both conditions), indicating that some models provided less reliable taxonomic judgments than others. However, models with comparable oracle performance showed substantially different success rates: for instance, MiniMax-M2.5, GLM-5, and GPT-5-Nano achieved broadly similar oracle κ\kappa scores (0.67–0.91, 0.78–0.76, and 0.81–0.72, respectively) yet succeeded on 35%, 58%, and 12% of games respectively. This dissociation suggested that oracle quality was not sufficient for player success. We analyzed this further below.

#### Disentangling Player Reasoning from Oracle Quality.

We aimed to better assess whether player strategy or oracle quality were determinant for the player’s success. To this end, we fit a Bayesian mixed-effects logistic regression at the game level (n=1,200n=1{,}200), with Confirmation bias and oracle error rate as fixed-effect predictors and LLM identity as a random effect.555See Appendix D.2 for complete model details and results.
Confirmation bias was used in the model as a continuous predictor in [0,1][0,1], representing the proportion of positive tests within a game. Oracle error rate was modeled as a binary variable, taking the value 1 if the oracle’s response disagreed with the human annotator on either the sampled Test or Guess turn for that game, and 0 otherwise.666Although an exhaustive human annotation of all turns across 1,200 games would enable us to calculate an exact continuous oracle error rate, we restricted our analysis to this binary estimate to keep the annotation effort manageable.
As shown in Figure 3 (left), Confirmation bias was a strong negative predictor of success (posterior mean =−4.15=-4.15, OR =0.016=0.016, posterior SD =0.116=0.116), while oracle error rate showed no credible independent effect (posterior mean =−0.12=-0.12, OR =0.885=0.885, posterior SD =0.147=0.147), with a credible interval spanning zero. Moreover, Figure 3 (right) showed near-identical predicted success curves across oracle error conditions, suggesting that failures were driven primarily by the model’s own Confirmation bias rather than by the quality of the oracle’s feedback. The residual between-model variance captured by the random effect (σ^u=0.553\hat{\sigma}_{u}=0.553) indicates that LLM identity provides additional explanatory information beyond the two predictors. This reflects the factors that differentiate the overall quality of LLMs.

##
5 In-depth Turn Level Analysis

The Confirmation bias reported in Section 4 measures the intent behind a model’s testing strategy, implicitly assuming that positive testing is a less optimal behavior than negative testing. In order to determine the effectiveness of a given strategy in rejecting a wrong hypothesis, it is necessary to understand the relationship between each current hypothesis HH and the target rule RR in games (see Table 1). Indeed, it is possible that the player would form hypotheses for which negative testing is suboptimal and positive testing should instead be favored. For this reason, we employed one of the models tested on FalsifyBench as an offline LLM annotator, which annotates all Test turns of each game across all models with the set relation between HH and RR. To balance speed and annotation cost, we selected GPT-5-Mini as annotator. The exact prompt used to perform this annotation can be seen in Appendix C.

#### Hypothesis-Target Relations.

Figure 4 shows the distribution of relations between HH and RR across all Test turns from all games for each model. The dominant configuration across all models is H⊂R (Hypothesis in Target), consistent with the assumption we made when building FalsifyBench: since the initial items are drawn from a semantic category SS which is a descendant of RR, models will form hypotheses that are more specific than the true target and revise them upward, yielding a sequence of H⊂R turns. For almost all models, the relation H⊂R was present in more than 50% of all turns, with the exception of GPT-5.2-Chat, which exhibited a lower share of H⊂R turns and a higher share of partial-overlap relations. Importantly, the H⊂R configuration is the only one in which negative testing is the uniquely optimal strategy, and it remained the most common throughout games. In the cases where HH is partially overlapping with or disjoint from RR, both positive and negative testing may lead to a falsification of HH (see Table 1).

#### Confirmation bias vs. Conclusive Falsification Rate.

Having access to the full annotation of the relation between HH and RR in each Test turn, we could check for each turn whether a positive or negative test would yield a falsification of HH. Thus, we measured the average proportion of tests yielding a falsification of HH across games, called conclusive falsification rate (Figure 5, top).
Reasoning models again outperformed instruction-tuned models on this measure, with GPT-OSS-20B (59.2%), GPT-OSS-120B (53.4%), and GPT-5.2-Chat (49.5%) achieving the highest rates. The lowest rates were again produced by Llama-4-Maverick (18.0%) and GPT-5-Nano (26.6%). As explained earlier, negative testing is not always guaranteed to be the better strategy for falsifying a wrong hypothesis; however, we show here that it was indeed mostly the case in FalsifyBench: Figure 5 (bottom) shows the Confirmation bias against the conclusive falsification rate across all models, revealing a strong negative correlation (Spearman ρ=−0.937\rho=-0.937, p<0.001p<0.001). The strength of this correlation supports the idea that testing negatively (i.e., having lower Confirmation bias) was a reliable proxy for reaching a conclusive falsification of wrong hypothesis in our framework.

##
6 Failure Beyond Confirmation Bias

As shown in the previous sections, Confirmation bias is associated with failure in FalsifyBench. Beyond this bias, which is related to the testing strategy players adopted, failure may also be attributable to the type of hypotheses they generated.

Failed games showed a systematically greater proportion of turns where the hypothesis was either disjoint from or partially overlapping with the target relative to successful games, indicating that models that failed did not follow a clear taxonomic path toward RR.777We show a full per-model decomposition of relation distributions across successful and failed games in Appendix E.2. Figure 6 (left) quantifies this difference by reporting, for each model, the mean gap in the proportion of partially overlapping and disjoint turns between failed and successful games. Positive values indicate that a given configuration is more prevalent in failed games. Asterisks denote statistically significant differences in the median number of turns of each type (Mann-Whitney U test, p<0.05p<0.05). Nearly all models exhibited a significantly higher proportion of partially overlapping turns in failed games, with reasoning models showing particularly pronounced differences (GPT-5.2-Chat: +27.0+27.0 pp, GPT-OSS-120B: +22.3+22.3 pp, GLM-5: +21.4+21.4 pp). This suggests that in failed games models frequently formed hypotheses that were similar to RR but failed to converge on the correct rule. Disjoint turns were also significantly more frequent in failed games for most models, though the effect was smaller in magnitude, indicating that while fully off-track hypotheses did occur, they were less prevalent than partially overlapping ones.

While these results provide evidence that, in failed games, models often did not fully grasp the taxonomic structure of the hypothesis space, we also aimed to qualitatively characterize the kinds of hypotheses they generated when diverging from the correct path. A notable case involved a focus on surface-level features of the words used in the game (e.g. “all items start with the letter a” or “all items have exactly three vowels in their names”) rather than on semantic properties (such as, “is a fish” or “is a mammal”). To detect such cases, we built a heuristic classifier based on regular expressions.888Additional details regarding the regular expression patterns used to build the classifier are reported in Appendix D.3. Figure 6 (right) shows the percentage of games, both successful and failed, in which at least one hypothesis, across all game turns, was classified as focusing on surface-level features of items. Models for which a Fisher’s exact test revealed a significantly higher proportion in failed games (p<0.05p<0.05) are denoted by an asterisk. This indicates that the tendency to form surface-level linguistic hypotheses was systematically associated with failure. Interestingly, for models with higher overall success rates, such as GLM-5, GPT-5.2-Chat and GPT-OSS-120B, nearly all failed games were characterized by the presence of at least one linguistic hypothesis (97.6%, 92.0% and 84.4% respectively). In contrast, weaker models such as GPT-5-Nano and Qwen3.5-9B showed no significant difference between successful and failed games (36.4% vs. 25.0% and 62.0% vs. 47.6% respectively), suggesting that these models generally struggled with the task and did not exhibit this specific failure mode.

##
7 Conclusion

We introduced FalsifyBench, a benchmark for evaluating hypothesis-driven reasoning in LLMs through a semantic generalization of the Wason 2-4-6 task. Our evaluation of 12 models showed that reasoning models are generally stronger scientific thinkers than instruction-tuned models. However, no model came close to optimal performance, highlighting substantial room for improvement in the inductive reasoning capabilities of current LLMs. The primary driver of success was found to be the capacity for negative testing: models that actively seek to falsify their hypotheses consistently outperform those that seek to confirm them, and our turn-level analysis showed that disconfirmatory behavior is a reliable proxy for progress toward the correct hypothesis.

Finally, we showed that failure is also tied to how models explored the hypothesis space. In failed games, models tended to drift away from the target, forming hypotheses that were either disjoint from or only partially overlapping with the target rule, and were significantly more likely to propose rules based on surface-level properties of words rather than semantic categories.

##
8 Limitations

Our analysis uses WordNet as a proxy for semantic taxonomic knowledge. Although WordNet is a well-established lexical resource created by expert lexicographers, it has several limitations. For example, its hierarchy reflects specific lexicographic decisions, it is English-centric, and it may contain basic semantic inconsistencies. We sought to overcome these limitations by focusing on concrete and fairly unambiguous superordinate categories.

Additionally, we collected human annotations for only one Test turn and one Guess turn per game. While this sampling strategy kept annotation effort manageable across 1,200 games, it forced us to operationalize oracle error as a binary variable. We acknowledge that this is a coarse approximation. Nevertheless, the high raw accuracy observed across models suggests that the probability of a randomly selected turn resulting in an incorrect oracle judgment is low. This means that our binary estimate is likely to have overestimated rather than underestimated the true oracle error rate, making our regression result conservative. Despite this, the effect of oracle error on player success remains negligible.

Finally, our analysis was inspired by the history of scientific discoveries, in which previously accepted hypotheses have been replaced by more comprehensive ones. We acknowledge, however, that this is only one aspect of the scientific process, and leave further exploration of this topic to future research.

Appendix A When Can a Hypothesis be Falsified?

We provide a detailed analysis of when a test yields a falsification depending on the relation between the current hypothesis HH and the target rule RR. Following Klayman and Ha (1987), we classify any test xx along two binary dimensions: whether x∈Hx\in H (the test is positive with respect to the hypothesis) and whether x∈Rx\in R
(the test is true with respect to the target). A test within HH is a positive test; a test outside HH is a negative test. Whether the oracle confirms or rejects it determines whether the test is true or false. This yields four cases, summarized in Table 2, each with a distinct informational value for hypothesis revision.

In the following sections, we discuss in each possible relation between HH and RR which of the four quadrants yield a conclusive falsification and which do not.

### Case 1: H=R (Identical)

When HH is identical to RR, both false positives and false negatives are impossible. Every member of HH is in RR (so no test within HH can receive “Do not conform”), and no member outside HH is in RR (so no test outside HH can
receive “Conform”). Trivially, holding the correct hypothesis means that it cannot be falsified and only a Guess action would end the game.

### Case 2: H⊂R (H too narrow)

When HH is a strict subset of RR, every member of HH is automatically in RR, making false positives impossible: tests within HH always receive “Conform”. The only route to falsification is a false negative – testing outside HH and receiving “Conform”, revealing that RR extends beyond HH and forcing upward revision. Crucially, not every negative test achieves this: testing outside HH and receiving “Do not conform” (true negative) is equally consistent with HH and provides no new information. A player relying exclusively on positive tests is therefore confined to true positives and can never falsify HH. This is the dominant relation in FalsifyBench.

### Case 3: H⊃R (H too broad)

When HH is strictly broader than RR, every member of RR is inside HH, making false negatives impossible: no test outside HH can receive “Conform”. The only route to conclusive falsification is a false positive – testing within HH and receiving “Do not conform”, revealing that HH must be narrowed. Notably, a positive test strategy is the correct approach in this configuration: testing within HH is the only way to a falsification. Crucially, not every positive test achieves this: testing within HH and receiving “Conform” (true positive) is equally consistent with HH and provides no reason to discard it.

### Case 4: HH and RR partially overlap

When HH and RR partially overlap but neither contains the other, both false positives and false negatives are accessible. A test within HH but outside RR yields “Do not conform” (false positive, falsification): only a subset of HH is compatible with RR, revealing that HH must be revised to exclude that region. A test within RR but outside HH yields “Conform” (false negative, falsification): RR extends beyond HH, revealing that HH must be revised to include that region. Thus, two ways of falsification are available in this configuration. However, two non-conclusive outcomes are also possible: testing within both HH and RR yields “Conform” (true positive, non-conclusive), and testing outside both HH and RR yields “Do not conform” (true negative, non-conclusive). In both cases the feedback is consistent with HH and provides no pressure to revise it in either direction.

### Case 5: HH and RR disjoint

When HH and RR share no members, every test within HH yields “Do not conform” (false positive, falsification): HH is entirely incompatible with RR and must be discarded. Every test within RR but outside HH yields “Conform” (false negative, falsification): RR extends into a region entirely outside HH, and HH needs to be revised. The only non-conclusive outcome is testing outside both HH and RR, which yields “Do not conform” (true negative, non-conclusive): the feedback is consistent with HH and provides no information about how to revise it. Finally, true positives are impossible in this configuration, since HH and RR share no members.

Appendix C Game Engine Details

###
C.1 Prompts

The prompts we used verbatim in all experiments are shown in Tables 5–7. In each prompt, double curly-brace placeholders (e.g. {{max_turns}}) are filled before each game. The player prompts are shown in Table 5; the oracle prompts are shown in Table 6. Additionally, Table 7 shows the prompt used for the turn level annotation of the relations between a current hypothesis and the target rule.

###
C.2 Agent Roles and Action Space

Each game began with exactly three positive examples presented to the player. The player was never shown the target rule RR or the sampling category SS directly; it observed only the three example items and the system prompt explaining the rules of the game (see Table 5).

The interaction proceeded as a sequence of turns between the player and the oracle. The player was responsible for proposing actions aimed at discovering the hidden property, while the oracle evaluated these actions and provided feedback according to the player’s last action.

On each turn, the player performed exactly one of two actions:

-
•

Test: propose a triple of items together with a hypothesis string and a reasoning trace. The action must be well-formed JSON matching the schema specified in the system prompt; malformed or schema-invalid submissions trigger a retry (see Appendix C.3) and are not recorded as turns.

-
•

Guess: submit a single property string as a final answer. A correct guess returns “Correct” and ends the game; an incorrect guess returns “Incorrect”, after which the player can continue with additional Test or Guess actions.

After receiving a valid player action, the oracle performed the following actions:

-
•

Feedback generation: for a Test action, determine whether the proposed items satisfy the target rule RR and return the string “Conform” if they do and “Do not conform” otherwise; for a Guess action, determine whether the proposed property is semantically equivalent to RR by returning the string “Correct” if it does and “Incorrect” otherwise.

-
•

Strategy classification: after every Test action, classify the player’s testing strategy as positive if the player tests examples expected to satisfy its current hypothesis, and negative if it tests examples outside the hypothesis in order to challenge it and explore broader alternatives. This step was performed only for later analysis and did not affect gameplay directly.

A game turn was defined as one accepted player action together with the oracle’s response. Invalid submissions and retry attempts did not increment the turn counter.

###
C.3 Retry Mechanism

The game engine employed two independent retry mechanisms to handle failures in producing valid JSON outputs, and none of these retry attempts were recorded as game turns. For player actions, if the engine received malformed JSON or an action that violated the schema, it re-prompted within the same turn requesting strict JSON output; after the first failure, up to three additional retries were permitted. For oracle judgments, each method was allowed up to two attempts: if the first response could not be parsed as JSON, the engine re-issued the prompt with an appended instruction (“IMPORTANT: Return ONLY a JSON object”) and then attempted to parse the second response. This numeber of retries was sufficient to complete all games with all tested models.

###
C.4 Memory and statefulness.

During the game interactions, the player was stateful: its conversation history accumulated all prior oracle feedback and its own previous actions. In contrast, the oracle was stateless: each call to the oracle was performed using a newly initialized prompt that provided only the elements necessary to collect the current judgement, with no access to information from earlier turns.

The most consistent finding is that “artifact” was by far the hardest target rule across all models and metrics: success rates were uniformly low (0–30% for most models), Confirmation bias was highest, and conclusive falsification rates were lowest. This was likely because artifact subcategories spanned a semantically heterogeneous space, making it harder to form hypotheses that generalized cleanly upward through the taxonomy. By contrast, “animal” was the easiest target for most reasoning models, with GPT-5.2-Chat achieving a 100% success rate and several others exceeding 75%.

Regarding average guesses per game, Llama-4-Maverick submitted an average of nearly 8 guesses across all target properties, far exceeding every other model and confirming that its strategy relied heavily on passive guessing rather than active evidence gathering regardless of the semantic domain. In the artifact category, well-performing models also showed elevated guessing behavior, suggesting that the semantic difficulty of this category induced more premature commitment across models. Turn efficiency broadly mirrored success rate patterns, with animal games being solved fastest by reasoning models and artifact games either taking the most turns or remaining unsolved within the turn limit, as indicated by empty cells in the turns heatmap for models with zero success in that category.

###
E.4 Decomposition by Outcome

Figure 13 reports the average guesses per game and Confirmation bias split by game outcome (Succeeded vs. Failed).

In terms of average guesses (left panel), failed games involved substantially more guesses than successful ones across all models. The effect was most pronounced for Llama-4-Maverick, which averaged 8.5 guesses per failed game compared to 3.8 in successful ones, and for GPT-5.2-Chat, GPT-OSS-120B, and GPT-OSS-20B, which all approached 4–5 guesses per failed game while remaining below 2 in successful ones. This suggests that models relied on guessing and “Incorrect” feedback to disconfirm hypotheses when they were not able to efficiently explore the hypothesis space.

Similarly, regarding Confirmation bias (right panel), failed games exhibited higher Confirmation bias than successful ones for nearly every model. The gap was largest for stronger reasoning models: GPT-5.2-Chat showed 20% bias in successful games versus 50% in failed ones, while GPT-OSS-20B showed 17% versus 53%. This provides further within-model evidence that negative testing was associated with success and represented the most efficient strategy for reaching the target.
