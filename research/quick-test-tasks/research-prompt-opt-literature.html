Prompt-optimizer papers point to a synthetic latent-rule task, not a borrowed benchmark

Twelve candidate tasks, scored against the quick-test rubric, converge on three buildable synthetic families.

whetstone-ai · 2026-07-21
Quick-test rubric Recommendations Landscape Prompt-opt literature

Which tasks do the optimizers we implement actually use?

DSPy COPRO/MIPROv2, GEPA, OPRO, APE, ProTeGi, PromptBreeder, and EvoPrompt, plus prominent 2024-2026 successors, are scored here for fit against the quick-test rubric: single LLM-call node with exact deterministic scoring (no sandbox), output bounded to MC or a few words, default prompt below a known ceiling with an incremental path, a synthetic/generatable pool of hundreds of contamination-resistant instances, diagnostic failures, few-shot demos that measurably help, and prompt differences resolvable on 10-20 evals at temperature 0.

The literature already points to the rubric's ideal task. The three best direct-fit synthetic families are multistep-arithmetic / word-sort generators (from BBH), a hidden-rule mapping task (from Instruction Induction), and a rule-based classifier (from the text-classification suite). All three deliver a known ceiling/floor, decomposable latent rules, exact few-word scoring, contamination resistance, and demo-learnability. Published headroom evidence for our exact optimizers comes from HotpotQA/HoVer/PUPA (COPRO/MIPROv2/GEPA show monotone, optimizer-separating gaps) and BBH/GSM8K (OPRO/EvoPrompt/PromptBreeder show large exact-match gaps); format templates come from Ethos/Liar/SST-2 and IFBench.

What do the cross-cutting patterns say before the candidate-by-candidate detail?

Most-used shared substrates

BIG-Bench Hard (BBH), GSM8K, the Instruction-Induction (II) and BBII suites, and a handful of text-classification sets (Ethos, Liar, SST-2, Subj, TREC) recur across OPRO, APE, ProTeGi, PromptBreeder, EvoPrompt, and PromptWizard.

DSPy-lineage tasks: best evidence, worst direct fit

COPRO, MIPROv2, and GEPA use multi-hop pipeline tasks (HotpotQA, HoVer, PUPA, IFBench) — exactly what our harness's optimizers demonstrably move, but they violate criteria 1-2 (retrieval, multi-node graphs, non-exact metrics). Adapt their headroom structure; do not reuse the task.

Where the headroom is cleanest

BBH symbolic sub-tasks and II/BBII latent-rule tasks sit closest to the rubric because they are algorithmic, exact-match, few-word-output, and decomposable. OPRO's up to +50% on BBH and PromptBreeder/EvoPrompt's BBH gains are the strongest published headroom signals on exact-match, single-call tasks.

Why everything published is contaminated

Every published benchmark here is contaminated to some degree, which is why the rubric wants synthetic generation. II tasks are algorithmically generatable, and BBH's symbolic sub-tasks (multi-step arithmetic, tracking shuffled objects, logical deduction, word sorting) are parameterized generators with exact ground truth — the template for our synthetic family.

Which synthetic families should get built first?

1 · BBH symbolic sub-tasks Excellent

Gains. OPRO reports up to +50% over human-designed prompts on BBH; EvoPrompt up to +25% (avg ~2.5-3.5%); TextGrad ~64.9 avg with Llama3-8B — large, clearly incremental exact-match gaps.

Fit. Multi-step arithmetic, tracking shuffled objects, logical deduction, and word sorting each decompose into independent latent rules (operator precedence, swap order, ordering constraints, lexicographic key), giving incremental score, diagnostic failures, and demos that plainly help.

Adaptation. Reimplement 1-2 sub-tasks (tracking-shuffled-objects, multi-step-arithmetic) as a seeded synthetic generator with a length/complexity knob (swap or operation count) doubling as the vestigial output-length metric.

2 · Instruction Induction (II) suite Very strong

Gains. APE reaches human-level on 24/24 tasks (IQM 0.765-0.810 vs human 0.749); on BBII it improves or matches zero-shot on 17/21 — a naive instruction scores far below a good induced one, which is exactly the incremental headroom the rubric wants.

Fit. Each task (larger-animal, second-letter, antonym, sum, pluralization…) is one latent rule, several trivially generatable with exact labels; few-shot demos are the whole premise.

Adaptation. Compose a multi-rule variant ("apply rule A then rule B") so difficulty decomposes into independent rules and partial prompts earn partial score. Generate hundreds per rule combo with an RNG seed.

10 · Text-classification suite → synthetic rule-based classifier Format ideal

Gains. EvoPrompt reports 8-22% accuracy gains over baselines across SST-2/classification/BBH; SAMMO reports >100% relative gains on some older-model cases.

Fit. Single call, MC/short-label output, exact match — ideal cheap format, but sentiment/topic are single latent constructs with poor rule decomposition and are contaminated as published.

Adaptation. Convert to a synthetic rule-based classifier: generate feature-bearing strings and assign the label by a composition of hidden Boolean rules ("positive iff token-class A and not token-class B"). Each rule adds accuracy as the prompt captures it — one of the top two synthetic-family designs.

11 · Multistep-arithmetic / word-sort generators Best fit

Gains. Inherits BBH's large exact-match gaps (see #1).

Fit. The model can be forced to emit only the final answer — truly bounded output, exact match, no sandbox. Difficulty knob = number of operations/list length; latent rules = operator precedence, associativity, lexicographic key, tie-handling; failures are diagnostic ("expected 42, got 48" → precedence rule missed).

Adaptation. Implement the generator plus answer-only scoring plus a length-budget knob as the vestigial second metric — minimal engineering, maximal rubric coverage.

12 · Symbolic hidden-rule mapping (fully synthetic) Excellent

Lineage. Generalizes Instruction Induction (#2) and MIR-Bench-style function I/O induction (Yan et al. 2025) — active 2025-2026 research interest in in-context rule induction.

Fit. Define K independent latent transformation rules (character substitution, positional swap, index arithmetic); each instance applies a seeded subset with an exactly computed ground truth. Prompt quality tracks how many rules it states — smooth incremental score, diagnostic failures, a known 100% ceiling and known floor.

Adaptation. Essentially the rubric's ideal task written from scratch: incrementality/decomposition from the II literature, exact-match/few-word format from BBH. Top candidate alongside #11.

Is there a task whose scoring mechanism is right even though its published gaps are thin?

7 · IFBench (instruction-following constraints) Partial-to-good

Gains. GEPA (Qwen3-8B): 36.9 → 36.2 → 38.6; (GPT-4.1-mini): 47.8 → 49.2 → 52.7 — small gaps, a caution that instruction-following alone may be too flat for clean ranking.

Fit. The scoring mechanism — programmatic constraint checking ("answer only yes/no", output-format constraints) — is an excellent rubric match even though the published headroom is thin.

Adaptation. Build a synthetic constraint-composition task: each instance imposes N independent, programmatically-checkable constraints (casing, length, forbidden words, required prefix, MC letter). Each constraint is one latent rule, giving incremental score and diagnostic "violated constraint X" failures. Closely aligned with #1/#2.

What are the cheapest single-call classifier formats to borrow?

8 · Ethos (hate-speech) → synthetic-family seed

ProTeGi reaches 0.95; PromptBreeder goes 80% (hand prompt) → 89% (evolved) — single call, binary label, exact match, the cheapest possible scoring. Weaknesses: one latent construct, contaminated, subjective labels. Use as the shape template for a synthetic binary/MC classifier where the label follows a hidden Boolean rule (see #10).

9 · Liar (fact-checking, 6-way) → ProTeGi evidence

ProTeGi reaches ~0.64 — a hard task with large residual headroom, which is the point. Format is good (MC, exact match, single call) but real-world content is contaminated with no clean latent-rule decomposition or known 100% ceiling. Borrow the multi-way categorical exact-match format for a synthetic classifier with a known ceiling.

Which tasks only prove our optimizers work, without fitting the rubric directly?

These tasks are the strongest published proof that our exact optimizers close gaps incrementally, but they violate criteria 1-2 (retrieval, multi-node graphs, long CoT, or non-exact metrics). Retained as citations, excluded as the quick-test task.

3 · GSM8K (grade-school math) Partial

OPRO 71.8% → 80.2% (PaLM-2-L, "+8% over human prompts"); PromptBreeder 83.9% zero-shot (evolved prompt "SOLUTION:") vs CoT 63.8% / Plan-and-Solve 65.4%; PromptWizard 90%. Scoring is deterministic (number match), but outputs are not bounded to a few words (CoT), the set is heavily contaminated, and it is a single latent skill with no clean rule decomposition. Forcing short-form answers collapses it into candidate #1/#11 — keep as a reference-gain data point.

4 · HotpotQA (multi-hop QA) Poor fit / strongest proof

The flagship DSPy-lineage task for COPRO, MIPROv2, and GEPA. GEPA paper (Qwen3-8B): baseline 42.3 → MIPROv2 55.3 → GEPA 62.3; (GPT-4.1-mini): 38.0 → 58.0 → 69.0 — clear, monotone, optimizer-separating gaps (a separate teleprompter comparison found the gains small vs cross-seed std; signal can be noisy). Poor direct fit (multi-node retrieval pipeline, multiple LLM calls violate criteria 1-2), but the single strongest published proof that our exact optimizers produce an incremental, monotone gap. Not adaptable without discarding what makes it HotpotQA — excluded as a task, retained as the "these optimizers close gaps incrementally" citation.

5 · HoVer (multi-hop claim verification)

GEPA (Qwen3-8B): 35.3 → MIPROv2 47.3 → 52.3; (GPT-4.1-mini): 46.3 → 48.3 → 51.7 — incremental, optimizer-separating, but multi-hop retrieval plus query writing (multi-call, scored on gold-document recall) fails criteria 1-2. Reference only, for MIPROv2/GEPA incrementality evidence.

6 · PUPA (privacy-conscious delegation)

GEPA (Qwen3-8B): 80.8 → 81.6 → 91.9; (GPT-4.1-mini): 78.6 → 83.4 → 94.5 — MIPROv2 barely moves while GEPA jumps, a good optimizer-discrimination example. Composite metric (quality + PII-leakage) is not exact-match, so it stays reference-only, though the two-objective aggregate is a useful conceptual template for a quality-vs-output-length knob.

What should the quick-test designer take away?

Best direct-fit synthetic families

#11 (multistep-arithmetic/word-sort generator), #12 (hidden-rule mapping), and #10-as-synthetic (rule-based classifier). All three give a known ceiling/floor, decomposable latent rules, exact few-word scoring, contamination resistance, and demo-learnability.

Best headroom evidence for our optimizers

HotpotQA/HoVer/PUPA (COPRO, MIPROv2, GEPA show monotone, optimizer-separating gaps) and BBH/GSM8K (OPRO/EvoPrompt/PromptBreeder show large exact-match gaps) justify expecting an incremental gap, even though none of these is itself the quick-test task.

Format templates

Ethos/Liar/SST-2 (cheap single-call MC/exact-match) and IFBench (programmatic constraint checking) for the scoring harness.
